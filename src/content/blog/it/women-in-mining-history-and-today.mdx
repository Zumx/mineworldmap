Women have always worked in mining — at the surface and sotterraneo — but the history is patchy, hidden and often deliberately erased. From medieval ore-sorters to modern engineers, the story is now being recovered.

## Medieval ore-sorting

Surface labour at most medieval European mines was overwhelmingly female. Picking, washing and sorting ore was women's work and is documented in Agricola's De Re Metallica (1556).

## Underground women in British coal

Before the 1842 Mines Act, women worked sotterraneo in British coal mines hauling sleds — sometimes naked because of heat. The Royal Commission of 1840–42 documented this with deliberately shocking testimony to force reform.

## The Pit Brow Lasses

After 1842 women continued to work above ground at British collieries — most famously the 'Pit Brow Lasses' of Wigan in their distinctive trousers (a Victorian scandal). They worked at the screens picking stone from coal.

## Cornish bal maidens

Cornish 'bal maidens' worked surface ore-dressing at tin and copper mines into the 1920s. The Bal Maidens Project (St Just in Penwith) has reconstructed the social history.

## Welsh tip-pickers

Welsh women and children gathered coal from spoil tips for domestic fuel into the 1950s — a parallel grey economy alongside the formal industry.

## Women in war work

Both world wars saw women work sotterraneo in jobs previously reserved for men. In the US, Rosie the Riveter has a less-famous mining counterpart in the Pennsylvania anthracite breaker girls of WWI.

## Apartheid and exclusion

South African legislation banned women from working sotterraneo until 1996. Lifting the ban opened previously male-only spaces — and triggered slow culture change.

## Modern mining engineering

Women now form around 15 % of mining engineering graduates worldwide and 8 % of sotterraneo workforces. The proportion is rising slowly. The Women in Mining UK organisation tracks the figures.

## Visible heritage

The Wigan Pit Brow Lasses statue, the Bal Maidens memorial at St Just, and the women's-history sections of Big Pit, the National Coal Mining Museum and Lewarde all interpret the female history of mining seriously.
