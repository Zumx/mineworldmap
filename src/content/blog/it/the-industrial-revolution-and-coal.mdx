No fuel did more to remake the world than coal. From the steam engines of 18th-century Britain to the rolling mills of the Ruhr and the railroads of the Appalachians, coal underpinned every form of industrial transformation.

## Before steam

Coal was burned in monastic forges and London households from the 13th century, but the volumes were small. Wood and charcoal remained the dominant industrial fuels into the 17th century, and the deforestation of England forced a slow but decisive shift to coal.

## Newcomen and Watt

Thomas Newcomen's 1712 atmospheric engine was built to pump water from coal pits in the West Midlands. James Watt's separate condenser (1769) improved efficiency enough to make steam a general-purpose power source — first in mines, then in everything else.

## The geography of the British coalfields

Industrialisation followed the seams. South Wales, Lanarkshire, Tyneside, South Yorkshire, the East Midlands and the Cumberland coast all became dense industrial regions. The 'pit village' became a recognisable settlement type.

## The transformation of work

Coal mining recruited men, women and children. Pre-1842 legislation, women hauled coal sleds sotterraneo; girls of seven operated ventilation trapdoors. The 1842 Mines Act ended these practices in Britain.

## Trade unions

The discipline of mass sotterraneo labour created some of the earliest industrial trade unions. The Miners' Federation of Great Britain (1888) was for decades the largest union in the world.

## Coal in the Ruhr

Continental Europe followed Britain at a half-century lag. The Ruhr coalfield, opened from the 1840s, fed the German rolling mills and produced the Krupp armaments empire. By 1913 the Ruhr produced 110 million tonnes of coal a year.

## Coal in the United States

American industrialisation ran on Pennsylvania anthracite, then West Virginia bituminous. The Mollie Maguires and the United Mine Workers of America (1890) wrote a parallel American chapter of industrial labour history.

## Globalisation of coal

By 1900 coalfields were industrially developed in Australia, India, China, South Africa and Japan. The exchange of mining engineers — many of them Cornish — was a key strand of late-Victorian globalisation.

## Decline and afterlife

Most western coalfields closed between 1960 and 2020. The Ruhr, South Wales and Appalachia each developed distinctive cultural responses to that decline. The world coal industry is, of course, far from extinct — production has shifted to China, India and Indonesia.
