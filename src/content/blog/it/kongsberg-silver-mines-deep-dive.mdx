From 1623 to 1958 the silver mines of Kongsberg produced 1.35 million kg of silver under royal Danish-Norwegian ownership. The visitor mine south-west of Oslo is one of the deepest accessible to the public anywhere in Europe.

## Scoperta 1623

Two children, Helga and Jacob, found a glittering vein on the slopes of Underberget on 16 July 1623. Their father reported it; King Christian IV of Denmark-Norway claimed the find within months.

## Royal monopoly

From 1624 the mines were a royal regalia — operated by the crown directly. The Sølvverket (Silver Works) became one of the largest industrial operations in early-modern Europe.

## The town

Kongsberg was founded in 1624 as a mining city. By 1770 it was the second-largest urban centre in Norway after Bergen, with 8000 inhabitants — most of them mineworkers and their families.

## Christian Stoll

The Kongens Gruve (King's Mine), reached via the 2.3 km Christian Stoll horizontal adit, is the visitor centrepiece. A small train carries visitors deep into the mountain.

## Native silver

Kongsberg is famous among mineralogists for spectacular crystalline native silver — wires, ferns and dendrites of pure metal extending up to a metre. The best specimens are in the Natural History Museum of Oslo.

## Working life

At the peak of operations 4000 men, women and children worked on the field. Wages were paid in 'Sølvverket dalers' redeemable only at company stores — a classic 17th-century truck system.

## The Royal Mint

Founded at Kongsberg in 1686, the mint stamped Danish-Norwegian, then Norwegian, coinage. It remains in operation today as Det Norske Myntverket, the oldest continuously operating mint in northern Europe.

## Chiusura

Silver extraction ended in 1958 after 335 years. The mine became a museum that same year.

## L'esperienza di visita

The Norsk Bergverksmuseum (Norwegian Mining Museum) in the town centre complements the sotterraneo tour, with displays on native silver, mint history and miners' life.
