The Rammelsberg, a 635-m hill at the foot of the Harz mountains, was mined for copper, lead, silver and zinc for 1000 years until 1988. Together with the medieval town of Goslar it forms a unique UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## Medieval origins

Mining at the Rammelsberg is documented from 968 and probably began earlier. The German emperor Otto I held court at Goslar precisely because of the mineral wealth below the hill.

## The Goslar Imperial Palace

Built 1040–1050 under Henry III, the Kaiserpfalz is one of the largest surviving Romanesque palaces in Europe. It anchored Salian-dynasty rule and was funded by ore from across the valley.

## The mining technology

The Rammelsberg pioneered drainage, ventilation and ore-washing techniques over centuries. The 'Roeder Stollen' (1805) preserved water-wheel galleries 75 m below the surface, still operable today.

## Polymetallic geology

The ore body is unusual: a single deposit yielded copper, lead, zinc, silver and gold over its history. By the 20th century lead and zinc were the main products.

## Chiusura and inscription

Active extraction ceased in 1988. The site was inscribed by UNESCO in 1992 — the first major industrial-monument inscription in Germany.

## The visitor mine

The 'Roeder Stollen' tour descends to working water-wheels still turning on diverted mountain streams. The 'Rathstiefster Stollen' offers more authentic sotterraneo textures.

## Surface exhibits

The dressing plant of 1936 is preserved as an active multi-storey machine still operable for demonstrations — one of the most extraordinary industrial dinosaurs anywhere.

## The town of Goslar

Inscribed separately and jointly with the Rammelsberg, Goslar's medieval centre, market church and frescoed houses survive almost untouched. The town museum extends the mining narrative.

## Wider Harz network

The 'Upper Harz Water Management System' was added to the UNESCO inscription in 2010 — 50 km of 17th-century artificial water-channels that powered the entire mining district.
