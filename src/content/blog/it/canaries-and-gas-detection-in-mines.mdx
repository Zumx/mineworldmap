From candle flames to electronic sensors, mining has needed ways to detect the gases that killed miners by the thousand. The canary was perhaps the most famous — and the most accidental — early-warning system.

## The gases

Coal mines accumulate methane (firedamp) — explosive; carbon monoxide (white damp) — toxic; carbon dioxide (black damp) — asphyxiating; and hydrogen sulphide (stinkdamp) — toxic. Each requires a different detection strategy.

## Candle flame

Before scientific detection, miners used candle flames as crude indicators. A blue flame above the candle indicated methane; a yellow or dying flame indicated carbon dioxide or carbon monoxide.

## The Davy lamp 1815

Humphry Davy's safety lamp surrounded the flame with a wire-gauze mesh that cooled escaping gases below the methane ignition point. The lamp transformed mining safety — and remained in use for nearly 200 years.

## Stephenson's lamp

George Stephenson developed a parallel safety lamp the same year. The priority dispute between the two was sharp; both designs were widely used.

## Canaries

The use of canaries was proposed by John Scott Haldane in 1895. Small birds have a respiration rate roughly twice that of mammals and react to carbon monoxide much faster. A swaying or fallen canary gave miners crucial minutes to evacuate.

## Canary practice

Canaries were carried in small cages and revived in a sealed chamber with an oxygen feed if they collapsed — a practice known as 'resuscitation by mouth' for the bird. British coal mines retained canaries until 1986.

## Modern electronic detection

From the 1970s electronic methane detectors, then multi-gas monitors, replaced the canary. Modern self-contained personal monitors detect methane, CO, CO₂ and H₂S to parts-per-million sensitivity.

## Mine rescue

The Mines Rescue Service in Britain (1902–) and similar organisations elsewhere developed self-contained breathing apparatus, the proto-form of modern firefighter equipment. Recipes — fixed-time air supplies — were carried by every miner from the early 20th century.

## Heritage

Big Pit, the National Coal Mining Museum at Wakefield, the Mining Museum at Bochum and the Scottish Mining Museum at Newtongrange all interpret gas detection and safety lamps in serious depth.
