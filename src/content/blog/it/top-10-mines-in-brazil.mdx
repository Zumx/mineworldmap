Il patrimonio minerario brasiliano is dominated by the colonial gold cycle of Minas Gerais — and by the iron-ore megamines of Carajás and the gemstone fields of Bahia.

## Ouro Preto (UNESCO)

A baroque colonial gold city, inscribed in 1980, with the Mina do Chico Rei and Mina da Passagem accessible to visitors.

## Mina da Passagem, Mariana

A 17th-century gold mine in Minas Gerais; the largest gold mine accessible to visitors in the world, with an inclined railway descending 120 metres.

## Diamantina (UNESCO)

A colonial diamond town, inscribed in 1999, with a mineralogical museum and surrounding garimpo trails.

## Mina do Chico Rei, Ouro Preto

Named for an enslaved African king who bought his freedom and that of others with gold from this very gallery.

## Carajás Iron Mine, Pará

Vale's flagship iron-ore mine, the world's largest single iron deposit, with very limited but possible visitor access.

## Serra Pelada Memorial, Pará

The infamous 1980s gold rush site documented by Sebastião Salgado; now an open-pit lake and memorial.

## Sabará, Minas Gerais

A small baroque gold town near Belo Horizonte, with the Museum of Gold.

## Lavras do Sul, Rio Grande do Sul

An 18th-century gold-and-copper field with surviving mine workings.

## Goiás Velho (UNESCO)

A colonial gold town in central Brazil, inscribed 2001.

## Minaçu and Niquelândia Nickel Mines

Goiás nickel district with a small museum.

## Pianificare l'itinerario

These ten sites span very different climates, languages and accessibility levels. Group them by region, allow a full half-day for each sotterraneo descent, and be ready for temperature fresche (around 8–12°C in most galleries). Local guides are often ex minatori — their commentary is the single best part of any visit.
