The 1886 discovery of gold on the Witwatersrand created Johannesburg and the modern South African state. The visitor mine at Gold Reef City, on a closed shaft of the Crown Mines, preserves a slice of that history.

## Géologie

The Witwatersrand Basin is a 2.7-billion-year-old sedimentary deposit covering an arc 350 km long. Gold occurs in narrow conglomerate beds — the 'reefs' — at depths from a few hundred metres to nearly four kilometres.

## Découverte 1886

The accepted discovery date is February 1886, by George Harrison on the farm Langlaagte west of present-day Johannesburg. By the end of that year the rush was on.

## Boer republic and Anglo-Boer Wars

The mineral wealth precipitated a colonial confrontation. The Second Boer War (1899–1902) was fought largely over control of Rand gold. The Union of South Africa (1910) was its outcome.

## The labour system

South African gold mining depended on migrant African labour from the start — recruited from across southern Africa, housed in compounds, paid a fraction of white wages. The system shaped apartheid before apartheid existed in law.

## Industrial scale

By the 1970s Witwatersrand mines reached depths of 3.5 km and employed 600 000 men. Total Rand gold output exceeds 50 000 tonnes — over a third of all gold ever mined.

## Decline

Production peaked in 1970 at 1000 t/year. Today the Rand produces a fifth of that. Many shafts have closed; illegal 'zama-zama' mining continues in abandoned workings.

## Gold Reef City

Established 1986 on Crown Mines Shaft 14, Gold Reef City combines a working souterrain tour to 226 m, a museum and an amusement park. The mine descent is the serious part; the park funds the heritage.

## Apartheid Museum

Adjoining Gold Reef City (separate entry), the Apartheid Museum opened in 2001 is widely regarded as one of the most important museums in Africa — telling the political history of the system that grew out of the mines.

## Wider Rand heritage

Other heritage sites include the City Deep mine memorial and the Sterkfontein hominid caves a short distance away — themselves shaped by mine drainage.
