Copper has shaped human history since the Chalcolithic. These landscapes — open pits, smelters, slag dumps — are vast and often hauntingly beautiful.

## Chuquicamata, Chile

The world's largest open-pit copper mine until recently overtaken by Bingham Canyon; visitor viewpoint over a 1 km-deep pit.

## Bingham Canyon, Utah, USA

A 1.2 km-deep open pit visible from space; visitor centre and viewing terrace at the rim.

## Falun, Sweden (UNESCO)

A medieval copper-mine landscape with the spectacular Great Pit collapse of 1687.

## Røros, Norway (UNESCO)

A wooden mining town and surrounding smelter ruins from 1646 onwards.

## Cornwall and West Devon, UK (UNESCO)

A coastal landscape of engine houses and copper-tin workings, inscribed 2006.

## Tonglushan, China

Bronze-Age copper workings now displayed as a national mining park.

## Sulitjelma, Norway

An Arctic copper-pyrite field above the Arctic Circle.

## Bisbee, Arizona, USA

A 1880s copper town with the spectacular Lavender open-pit.

## Calumet–Hancock, Michigan, USA

Lake Superior native-copper district, with the Quincy Mine hoist as a national landmark.

## El Teniente, Chile

The world's largest souterrain copper mine, with limited visitor access.

## Préparer la visite

Copper landscapes reward the long view — many are best seen from a viewing platform rather than souterrain. La plupart des sites disposent de centres d'accueil open from spring to autumn and offer guided descents in English in summer.
