Mercury was the indispensable companion to colonial silver — used in amalgamation to refine American ores. Two UNESCO sites tell the whole story.

## Almadén, Spain (UNESCO)

The world's largest mercury reserve, in continuous extraction from the Romans to 2003; UNESCO-inscribed jointly with Idrija in 2012.

## Idrija, Slovenia (UNESCO)

Europe's second-largest mercury producer, in operation 1490–1995; preserved Anthony's Shaft and miner's house.

## Huancavelica, Peru

Colonial Spanish mercury source for the Potosí silver — Santa Bárbara mine, UNESCO-inscribed 2024.

## Wanshan, Guizhou, China

The 'mercury capital of China'; closed 2002, now a national mining park.

## New Almaden, California

Spanish-Mexican mercury district in California, now a county park and museum.

## Monte Amiata, Tuscany, Italy

Italian mercury heritage with several restored mines in southern Tuscany.

## McDermitt, Nevada / Oregon, USA

Late-20th-century mercury district with a small museum.

## Khaidarkan, Kyrgyzstan

One of the world's last working mercury mines; visitor access is limited but exists.

## Khan Tengri Mining Museum, Bishkek

Comprehensive Central Asian mining heritage including mercury.

## Belknap and Sulphur Bank, California

Historic Californian mercury fields with surface ruins.

## Préparer la visite
