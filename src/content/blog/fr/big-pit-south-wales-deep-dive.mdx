Big Pit at Blaenavon is the UK's most-visited mining heritage site — a real working colliery preserved exactly as it was when it closed in 1980. Former miners guide every souterrain tour.

## Origines

Big Pit opened in 1880 as the deepest shaft on the South Wales coalfield, replacing the cluster of small drifts that had served the Blaenavon ironworks since 1789.

## Production

At peak in the early 20th century the colliery employed 1300 men and produced 250 000 tonnes of steam coal a year, mainly for export to the Royal Navy via the docks at Newport.

## Fermeture and rebirth

Big Pit closed in 1980 and reopened in 1983 as a museum. The National Museum of Wales took it over in 2001; admission has been free since 2004.

## The souterrain tour

Visitors descend the original 90-metre shaft in the cage, the same cage that lifted men and coal for a century. The tour passes the stables, the haulage road and the coal face.

## Former miners as guides

Every tour is led by a man who actually worked here — a key part of what makes Big Pit work. The lived memory is irreplaceable, and the guides are forthright about pneumoconiosis, accidents and union politics.

## UNESCO landscape

The wider Blaenavon Industrial Landscape was UNESCO-inscribed in 2000 — encompassing not just the colliery but the ironworks, the railways and the workers' housing.

## Surface exhibits

The pit-head baths, lamp room and winding house are all in their working state. The 'King Coal: the Mining Experience' multimedia gallery interprets the social history.

## Blaenavon Ironworks

A 15-minute walk from Big Pit, the Cadw-managed ironworks (1789) is one of the best-preserved examples of an early industrial-revolution iron operation in the world.

## Conseils pratiques

Temperature souterrain is 10 °C; warm clothing essential. The tour is around 50 minutes; under-fives can ride but cannot enter the workings. Free parking, café and shop on site.
