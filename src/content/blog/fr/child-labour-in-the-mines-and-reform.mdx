For most of mining history, children worked souterrain. The 1842 British Mines Act marked the start of a long, uneven reform process — one still incomplete in parts of the world today.

## The pre-industrial pattern

Children worked in family mining operations across Europe from medieval times. Tasks included ore-sorting at the surface, leading pit ponies, and operating ventilation trapdoors.

## The British coalfields 1800–1842

Industrial-scale child labour was endemic in British coal mining by 1840. The Royal Commission of 1840–1842 documented children of five and six working twelve-hour shifts; girls of seven 'trapping' (operating ventilation doors); women hauling sleds souterrain with chains.

## The 1842 Mines Act

Lord Ashley's Act banned women and girls souterrain, and boys under 10. Inspection was sparse and enforcement spotty for decades, but the principle of state intervention in mine labour was established.

## Continental and American patterns

Belgium, France and Germany followed Britain at staggered intervals. The United States introduced state-level laws between the 1880s and 1910s; the Federal Coal Mine Safety Act came only in 1952.

## The breaker boys

American anthracite operations employed 'breaker boys' — children as young as eight — to pick slate from coal on conveyor belts above ground. Famous photographs by Lewis Hine in 1908–1911 helped end the practice.

## Cultural depictions

Émile Zola's Germinal (1885), D.H. Lawrence's Sons and Lovers (1913) and the songs collected by A.L. Lloyd from the Durham coalfield record what the official inquiries documented in colder language.

## ILO conventions

The International Labour Organisation's Minimum Age Convention (1973) and Worst Forms of Child Labour Convention (1999) frame the modern legal regime.

## Aujourd'hui's reality

The ILO estimates around 1 million children worldwide still work in mining and quarrying, especially in artisanal gold and cobalt operations in the Democratic Republic of Congo, in mica in India and in coal in Pakistan. Reform is incomplete.

## Heritage interpretation

Big Pit, the National Coal Mining Museum at Wakefield, Beamish and Lewarde all interpret child labour seriously. The Anthracite Heritage Museum in Scranton and the Tenement Museum in New York extend the American story.
