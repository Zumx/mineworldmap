When Cornish tin mining collapsed in the second half of the 19th century, perhaps 250 000 Cornish miners emigrated. 'Cousin Jacks' worked from Wisconsin to the Witwatersrand and left a global cultural trail.

## The Cornish crisis

Cornish tin mining peaked around 1860. Falling tin prices, mechanisation and the rise of overseas competition collapsed the industry between 1866 and 1900. Whole villages emigrated.

## Why Cornish miners?

Cornish hard-rock mining technique was the most advanced in the world for centuries. The 'Cornish pump' beam-engine, the 'Cornish square sets' of souterrain timbering, and the use of dynamite all spread from Cornwall to the global goldfields.

## Wisconsin and Iowa

The first large Cornish emigration was to the lead mines of southwest Wisconsin and Iowa in the 1830s and 1840s. Mineral Point, Wisconsin, preserves Cornish stone houses and a 'pendarvis' restored Cornish cottage row.

## California and Nevada

California's gold rush and the Nevada silver rushes (Comstock Lode, 1859) drew large Cornish populations. Grass Valley, California, remained 60 % Cornish into the 20th century.

## Michigan copper country

Calumet and Hancock on the Keweenaw Peninsula attracted thousands of Cornish from the 1840s. The Calumet Theatre, Italianate stone churches and the Quincy Mine all reflect Cornish capital and labour.

## Australian goldfields

Ballarat, Bendigo and Moonta in South Australia all had large Cornish populations. Moonta and Burra in South Australia together formed 'Little Cornwall' from the 1860s. The annual Kernewek Lowender festival celebrates the connection.

## South African Rand

Cornish miners brought their skills to the Witwatersrand from the late 1880s. The pneumonia epidemics in the Rand compounds devastated Cornish communities — death rates of 30 % a year — and led to the 1903 Cornish 'Phthisis' tragedy.

## Mexico and Peru

Real del Monte in Hidalgo, Mexico, became a Cornish enclave in the 1820s. Cornish pasties — paste — remain a Mexican specialty in the town. Peruvian and Bolivian mines also recruited Cornish foremen.

## Heritage today

The Cornwall and West Devon Mining Landscape was UNESCO-inscribed in 2006 — including not just the Cornish sites but the cultural-influence sites overseas. The Cornish diaspora is now formally recognised by the British government.
