Edward Hargraves's discovery near Bathurst in 1851 set off a series of Australian gold rushes that doubled the colony's population in a decade. The rushes shaped Victorian and New South Wales society lastingly.

## The Bathurst rush 1851

Edward Hargraves, recently returned from California, found gold at Ophir near Bathurst in February 1851. The Sydney rush followed within weeks; the New South Wales government formalised licences within months.

## Victoria and Ballarat

Larger deposits were found at Ballarat, Bendigo, Castlemaine and Mount Alexander later in 1851. Victoria's population exploded from 77 000 to 540 000 between 1851 and 1861. Melbourne briefly became the richest city in the world.

## The Eureka Stockade 1854

Resentment of the gold-licence system led to armed rebellion at Ballarat on 3 December 1854. 22 miners and 5 soldiers died. The defeat became a founding event in Australian democratic mythology — and led to the abolition of the licence and the introduction of a mining franchise.

## Chinese miners and the lambing-flat riots

By 1861 over 38 000 Chinese miners were working the Victorian fields. Anti-Chinese riots — most notoriously at Lambing Flat (1860–1861) — and discriminatory poll taxes followed. The seeds of the later White Australia policy were sown here.

## Methods

Alluvial work dominated the first decade; deep-lead and hard-rock mining followed from the late 1850s. The Cornish 'Cousin Jacks' brought tin-mining technology — including the 'Cornish pump' — to the Ballarat deep leads.

## Western Australia: Coolgardie and Kalgoorlie

Gold was found at Coolgardie in 1892 and Kalgoorlie in 1893. The Golden Mile at Kalgoorlie remained one of the world's richest small areas for a century. Charles Yelverton O'Connor's 1903 Goldfields Water Supply Scheme remains an engineering marvel.

## Indigenous Australians

Aboriginal communities across the goldfields were dispossessed, often violently. The 1860s Coranderrk Aboriginal Station controversy is one strand of an emerging history of resistance and survival.

## Heritage

Sovereign Hill at Ballarat is a full-scale reconstruction of a 1850s gold-rush township and Australia's most-visited industrial-heritage site. The Eureka Centre, Bendigo's Central Deborah Gold Mine, and Kalgoorlie's Super Pit and museum extend the route.

## Cultural legacy

Henry Lawson, Banjo Paterson and Marcus Clarke each wrote the Australian gold rushes into the national literature. The egalitarianism, mateship and democratic instincts of the rushes remain part of Australian self-understanding.
