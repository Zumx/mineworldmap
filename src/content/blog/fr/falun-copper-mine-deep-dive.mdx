The Great Copper Mountain at Falun produced two-thirds of Europe's copper at its 17th-century peak, financed Sweden's brief age of military greatness, and gave its name to a paint colour that still defines the Scandinavian landscape.

## Medieval origins

Copper extraction at Falun is documented from the 13th century. A 1288 deed of share transfer is the world's earliest evidence of corporate share ownership.

## The 17th-century boom

By the mid-1600s Falun produced two-thirds of Europe's copper. Royal taxes on the mine funded the wars of Gustavus Adolphus and Charles XII — the brief Swedish Baltic empire was largely financed from this single hole in the ground.

## The Great Pit

On Midsummer Day 1687 a vast section of the mine collapsed into a 95-m-deep open pit. Miraculously, because it was a feast day, no one was killed. The Great Pit remains the most spectacular feature of the surface site.

## Falu rödfärg

The mine's iron oxide waste, fired in roasters, produces 'Falu red' — the deep red paint that has covered Swedish wooden houses since the 16th century. Production continues today on the original site.

## Working life and death

Output was bought at the cost of extraordinary occupational hazard: rock-falls, burns from roasting works, and respiratory disease. The fossilised body of 'Fet-Mats' Israelsson, killed in a 1670 collapse and preserved in vitriol, was found and displayed in 1719.

## Fermeture and museum

Active copper extraction continued for over a thousand years; the mine closed in 1992. UNESCO inscription followed in 2001.

## L'expérience de visite

Visitors descend 67 m to the Creutz Shaft, walking through galleries that retain medieval tool marks. The surface museum is excellent on the metallurgical and social history.

## The town of Falun

The 17th-century town that grew around the mine retains its quirky red-painted wooden architecture and a beautifully preserved central square.

## Wider Bergslagen heritage

Falun anchors the central Swedish Bergslagen iron- and copper-mining region — combine with Sala silver mine and Engelsberg ironworks (also UNESCO) for a deep heritage week.
