Since the 1980s UNESCO has inscribed dozens of mining landscapes — coal, copper, silver, mercury, slate, salt — onto the World Heritage List. Together they form one of the most coherent global heritage networks.

## Wieliczka and Bochnia (1978)

Polish royal salt mines from the 13th century — among the first cultural inscriptions of any kind.

## Røros (1980)

A Norwegian wooden copper-mining town, including the smelter ruins and the surrounding cultural landscape.

## Potosí (1987)

The Bolivian silver mountain. On the Danger List since 2014 because of continued working.

## Rammelsberg & Goslar (1992)

A thousand-year polymetallic ore mine and a medieval imperial town in Germany.

## Las Médulas (1997)

Roman gold workings in León province, Spain — extraordinary opencast remnants of the ruina montium hydraulic technique.

## Hallstatt-Dachstein (1997)

Austrian salt landscape including the world's oldest salt mine.

## Diamantina and Ouro Preto (Brazil, multiple dates)

Colonial Brazilian gold and diamond towns, both inscribed in the 1980s and 1990s.

## Zollverein (2001)

The Bauhaus-influenced Ruhr coal mine in Essen.

## Blaenavon (2000)

The South Wales coal-and-iron landscape including Big Pit.

## Sewell (2006)

The Chilean copper-mining 'city of stairs' on the slopes above El Teniente.

## Cornwall and West Devon (2006)

The complete tin-and-copper landscape including engine houses, ports and worker housing.

## Nord-Pas-de-Calais coalfield (2012)

The French coal-mining landscape with 51 spoil tips and 21 headframes.

## Almadén and Idrija (2012)

Joint inscription of the world's two great mercury producers.

## Erzgebirge / Krušnohoří (2019)

The trans-boundary German-Czech mining landscape — 800 years of silver, tin and uranium extraction.

## Sawahlunto (2019)

A Dutch colonial coal town in West Sumatra.

## Slate Landscape of North-West Wales (2021)

Welsh slate quarries — a different mineral but the same industrial-landscape pattern.

## Roșia Montană (2021)

Roman gold workings in the Apuseni mountains of Romania, inscribed after a long civic battle.

## Huancavelica (2024)

The Spanish-colonial mercury supply for Potosí silver.
