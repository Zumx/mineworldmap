Mining safety is mostly written in tombstones. The major reforms in mining law worldwide have followed major disasters — the Senghenydd explosion, Monongah, Courrières, Aberfan, Marcinelle and others.

## Felling 1812 (England)

92 men and boys died in a firedamp explosion at the Felling colliery near Newcastle. The Reverend John Hodgson's published account triggered the Society for Preventing Accidents in Coal Mines — and led directly to Davy's safety lamp.

## Courrières 1906 (France)

1099 miners died in a coal-dust explosion in northern France — the worst mine disaster in European history. International mine-rescue cooperation followed; British, German and Italian teams arrived to assist.

## Monongah 1907 (USA)

362 dead in West Virginia, the worst mine disaster in American history. Combined with the Darr (1907) and Cherry (1909) disasters, it led to the establishment of the US Bureau of Mines in 1910.

## Senghenydd 1913 (Wales)

439 miners died in a methane explosion in South Wales — the worst British mine disaster ever. Penalty for safety violations: £10 (£24 per life lost).

## Mitsubishi Hojyo 1914 (Japan)

687 dead in the worst Japanese mine disaster, in Kyushu. Japanese mine safety law followed.

## Benxihu 1942 (Manchuria/Japan)

1549 dead in a Japanese-operated colliery in Manchuria — the worst mine disaster in world history. Most victims were Chinese forced labour. Almost no contemporary investigation.

## Aberfan 1966 (Wales)

A coal-tip collapse killed 144 people in Aberfan, including 116 children at Pantglas Junior School. The Mines and Quarries (Tips) Act 1969 followed; the public inquiry was unusually scathing.

## Marcinelle 1956 (Belgium)

262 miners died at the Bois du Cazier — most of them Italian guest workers. The disaster reshaped Belgian-Italian migration relations and led to European-wide mine safety harmonisation.

## Pike River 2010 (New Zealand)

29 dead in a methane explosion on the South Island. The Royal Commission report (2012) led to a new Health and Safety at Work regime across all NZ extractive industries.

## Marikana 2012 (South Africa)

34 striking platinum miners killed by police at Marikana — a different form of mining death, but a defining moment of South African post-apartheid labour politics.
