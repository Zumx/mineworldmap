Mining heritage travel is one of the most underrated forms of cultural travel —
dramatic landscapes, extraordinary engineering, and human stories. It just
needs planning around access and the realities of going underground.

## Step 1: Pick a mining region

Mining clusters by geology and history: the German Erzgebirge, Cornwall's tin
coast, the Ruhr coalfield, Colorado's mineral belt, the Bolivian altiplano.
Choose one and go deep. Use the [map](/map) to see where sites actually
concentrate.

## Step 2: Separate visitor mines from active and ruined ones

Three categories, very different trips: organised *visitor mines* (guided
underground), surface heritage and museums (open-pit overlooks, machinery,
ghost towns), and abandoned workings (look only, never enter). Plan mostly
around the first two; the third is photography from a safe distance.

## Step 3: Map and cluster

Open the [map](/map), filter to your region, and group sites into day-sized
clusters. A mining landscape often packs a museum, a show mine, and a heritage
railway within a short drive — a perfect single day.

## Step 4: Book underground tours early

Show-mine tours have hard capacity limits (small cages, fixed group sizes) and
sell out in season. Reserve the underground experiences first and build the
day's surface sites around those fixed times.

## Step 5: Plan for the conditions

Underground is cool and damp year-round (often 8–12°C). Pack a warm layer and
closed grippy footwear for every mine day regardless of the weather above.
Check duration, steps, and accessibility — routes vary from level walks to
ladders.

## Step 6: Front-load the underground

Do the physically demanding mine in the morning while fresh, then museums and
landscape in the afternoon. Two underground tours in one day is tiring and
often redundant; one descent plus surface heritage is the better rhythm.

## Step 7: Mind seasons and remote access

Mountain and ghost-town sites open only when roads clear; some are long rough
drives with no services. Build buffer time and fuel/water margins for remote
heritage, and confirm seasonal opening the week before.

## Step 8: Travel respectfully

Never enter abandoned workings — collapse and bad air kill explorers every
year. Collect no minerals. Treat the sites as memorials to brutal labour;
support the heritage associations that keep them open by paying for tours and
buying locally.

## Put it together

Region, then site type, then cluster, then booked underground tours, then
conditions. Open the [map](/map), draw the cluster, lock the tour times, and a
mining trip becomes a genuinely unforgettable kind of travel.
