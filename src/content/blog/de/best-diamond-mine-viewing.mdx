Diamond mines are usually closed to the public, but a remarkable number of viewpoints, fee-dig parks and museums make the industry visible.

## Crater of Diamonds State Park, Arkansas

The world's only diamond field open to the public for fee-digging — keep what you find.

## Kimberley Big Hole, South Africa

The largest hand-dug excavation in the world (1871–1914); 215 m deep and now a museum and viewing platform.

## Mirny Open Pit, Yakutia, Russia

A 525 m-deep ice-and-kimberlite pit visible from a perimeter viewpoint.

## Diavik, Northwest Territories, Canada

Sub-arctic island diamond mine; limited fly-in tourism.

## Jwaneng, Botswana

World's richest diamond mine by value; controlled visitor access.

## Diamantina, Brazil (UNESCO)

Colonial diamond town; the Garimpo do Tijuco trail follows historic workings.

## Argyle, Western Australia (closed 2020)

Source of the world's pink diamonds; visitor access ended with closure but Kununurra museum interprets the site.

## Karowe, Botswana

Origin of the Lesedi La Rona and Sewelô diamonds, the largest cut in the modern era.

## Yubileynaya Pipe, Yakutia

Older Siberian kimberlite pipe with a perimeter view.

## Royal Asscher Diamond Museum, Amsterdam

Cutting-house heritage; the Asscher firm cut the Cullinan, the largest diamond ever found.

## Tourplanung

Diamond mining is a heavily secured industry — most experiences are at the museum and viewing-platform level rather than unter Tage. Die meisten Stätten verfügen über Besucherzentren open from spring to autumn and offer guided descents in English in summer.
