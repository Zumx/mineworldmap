The 1886 discovery of gold on the Witwatersrand made Johannesburg, broke the Boer Republic and shaped the South African 20th century. It remains the largest single goldfield ever worked.

## Entdeckung 1886

George Harrison, an Australian prospector, is credited with the find on the farm Langlaagte in February 1886. Local rumour spread within days; the formal proclamation of the goldfield followed in October.

## The geology

The Witwatersrand Basin is a 2.7-billion-year-old sedimentary arc 350 km long. Gold occurs in narrow conglomerate beds at depths from 100 m to 4 000 m. Total contained gold is estimated at over 80 000 tonnes — perhaps 40 % of all gold ever mined.

## Johannesburg's birth

The mining camp at Ferreirasdorp grew into Johannesburg, named in 1886. By 1900 it had 100 000 inhabitants; by 1936 a million. It remains South Africa's largest city.

## The Boer Republic

Gold was on the territory of the Zuid-Afrikaansche Republiek under Paul Kruger. The Republic's struggle to control mining capital — and the Uitlander franchise question — were the immediate triggers of the Second Anglo-Boer War.

## The Anglo-Boer War 1899–1902

Britain's military victory was a costly affair: 22 000 British military deaths, 25 000 Boer civilian deaths (mostly in concentration camps), perhaps 20 000 black African deaths. The Treaty of Vereeniging ended the Boer republics.

## Migrant labour

From the start, Rand gold mining relied on migrant African labour recruited from across southern Africa — Mozambique, Lesotho, Malawi, Botswana. The compound system isolated workers and depressed wages. This structure shaped apartheid before apartheid was law.

## Industrial peak

By 1970 Rand mines reached 3 500 m depth; total employment exceeded 600 000. Annual gold output peaked at over 1 000 tonnes — three quarters of world production.

## Decline

Rand gold output has fallen by 90 % since 1970. Many shafts have closed; illegal zama-zama miners continue in abandoned workings, with regular fatalities.

## Visiting the heritage

Gold Reef City (on Crown Mines Shaft 14), the adjoining Apartheid Museum, and the Cradle of Humankind UNESCO site — itself a function of mine-dewatering exposing limestone caves — form the core of present-day Rand heritage tourism.
