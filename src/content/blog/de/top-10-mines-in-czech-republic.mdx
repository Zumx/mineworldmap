Das tschechische Bergbau-Erbe spans medieval silver towns, royal mints, and the bohemian uranium belt — with several UNESCO listings.

## Kutná Hora Silver Mines (UNESCO)

A 13th-century silver town that minted the Prague groschen — the standard medieval European coin. The Hrádek mine and St Barbara's church top any visit.

## Erzgebirge / Krušnohoří (UNESCO, 2019)

A trans-boundary mining landscape shared with Saxony, inscribed for 800 years of silver and tin extraction.

## Příbram Mining Museum

A 13th-century silver-and-uranium mining district; the museum sits on the former Vojtěch shaft, the first to reach a kilometre depth in the world (1875).

## Březové Hory, Příbram

An open-air mining complex with three preserved headframes and a steam winding engine.

## Jáchymov (Joachimsthal)

The 16th-century silver mining town that gave the world the word 'thaler' — ancestor of the dollar — and later became the first uranium-mining site in the world.

## Landek Park, Ostrava

The largest mining museum in the Czech Republic, on the former Anselm Pit in the Ostrava–Karviná coalfield.

## Důl Michal, Ostrava

A preserved Art Nouveau coal mine of 1843–1994, with the pit-bath and lamp room kept exactly as on closing day.

## Jeroným Mine, Čistá

A medieval tin mine with original 16th-century hand-cut galleries near the German border.

## Kladno Mining District

Central Bohemian coalfield with the Mayrau pit museum and a working narrow-gauge mining tram.

## Bohemian Garnet Mines, Podkrkonoší

Pyrope-garnet workings that supplied the celebrated Bohemian-garnet jewellery of the 19th century.

## Routenplanung

Diese zehn Stätten umfassen sehr unterschiedliche Klimazonen, languages and accessibility levels. Gruppieren Sie sie nach Region, allow einen halben Tag for each unter Tage descent, and be ready for kühle Temperaturen (around 8–12°C in most galleries). Die örtlichen Führer sind häufig pensionierte Bergleute — their commentary is the single best part of any visit.
