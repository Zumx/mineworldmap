From flint hand-picks to compressed-air rock-drills, the tools of mining have evolved over ten thousand years. Their archaeology is one of the most readable records of human technology.

## Stone Age picks

Antler picks and flint scrapers are documented from Neolithic flint mines such as Grimes Graves (Norfolk, c. 2700 BC) and Spiennes (Belgium, UNESCO-inscribed). The technique was extraction by undercutting and prying loose.

## Bronze Age tools

Bronze picks, hammers and chisels appear in copper mines such as Mitterberg (Austria, c. 1500 BC). The tools and the metal extracted with them are the same — a technological tautology.

## Roman mining tools

Roman mining used iron picks, sledgehammers and wedges. Fire-setting — heating the rock and dousing it with vinegar to crack it — was a key technique. Pliny describes both at Las Médulas.

## Medieval European tools

The 16th-century treatise De Re Metallica by Georgius Agricola (1556) illustrates a comprehensive set of medieval European mine tools: picks, sledgehammers, wedges, mauls, gads, hauling buckets and the wooden 'Bergmann's stick'.

## Gunpowder

Gunpowder was first used in mining at Banská Štiavnica in 1627. The technique transformed productivity — but introduced new dangers including premature explosions and gas hazards.

## Cornish square sets and timbering

The Cornish-developed square-set timbering system allowed safe extraction in unconsolidated ore — a critical technology export from the 1840s onwards.

## Compressed-air drills

Pneumatic rock drills (Burleigh, 1866; Ingersoll, 1871) transformed hard-rock mining. The Saint-Gotthard, Frejus and Hoosac tunnels were all driven with them.

## Dynamite 1867

Nobel's dynamite (1867) replaced gunpowder. The technological combination of compressed-air drilling and dynamite blasting defined the late-19th-century rush economies.

## Modern continuous miners

From the 1940s the continuous miner — a rotating-drum cutting machine — replaced individual drilling in coal. Longwall shearer machines now extract entire coal faces autonomously.

## Heritage tool collections

The Bergbaumuseum Bochum, the Royal Mining Museum at Sala (Sweden) and the Mining Museum at Wakefield each hold serious historical tool collections.
