Russia's mining geography is inseparable from its imperial and Soviet history.
The tsarist discovery of Siberia's resources drove eastward expansion; the
Soviet command economy mobilised those resources using forced labour at a scale
and brutality that shaped the physical geography of entire regions. Today Russia
is one of the world's largest producers of nickel, diamonds, palladium, coal,
and gold. Access to most active operations is restricted; some heritage sites
are accessible; others are toxic wastelands or state security zones.

## 1. Mir Diamond Mine, Mirny, Yakutia

The Mir pipe is one of the largest and deepest open-pit diamond mines in the
world: 525 metres deep, 1,200 metres across. Diamond commodity; open-pit
production 1957–2001, transitioned to underground (Irelyakh underground mine)
in 2009, partially flooded and operations suspended after 2017 accident. The
open pit creates local aerodynamics so dangerous to helicopters that overflights
were banned during the operational era. The town of Mirny — reached by air
from Yakutsk — grew around the mine and still exists as a one-industry
community. The flooded pit is visible on satellite imagery and from the town rim.

## 2. Norilsk Nickel Complex, Krasnoyarsk Krai

Norilsk is the world's largest nickel and palladium producer and one of the
most polluted cities on Earth. Nickel-copper-palladium commodity; operated by
Nornickel (formerly NORNIKEL), continuously active. The city is a closed zone
requiring prior permission for foreigners to visit. It was built almost entirely
by Gulag labour between 1935 and 1956: the Norillag camp complex at peak held
around 70,000 prisoners. The 2020 Norilsk oil spill — 21,000 tonnes of diesel
into the Ambarnaya River, one of the largest in Russian history — demonstrated
the operational and environmental risks of infrastructure maintained in
permafrost.

## 3. Karabash Copper Smelter, Chelyabinsk Oblast

Karabash was identified in a 1996 Russian government assessment as the most
polluted city in Russia. Copper commodity; the smelter has operated in various
forms since 1910, currently operated by RMK (Russian Copper Company).
The immediate surroundings of the smelter are devoid of vegetation for several
kilometres — a result of continuous sulphur dioxide emissions. The slag heaps,
the dead zone around the plant, and the contaminated Sak-Elga River are
visible and photographed extensively by journalists and documentary makers.
Karabash is cited in environmental education as a reference example of
unregulated industrial pollution.

## 4. Kola Superdeep Borehole, Murmansk Oblast

The Kola Superdeep Borehole (KSDB-3) was the world's deepest borehole for
24 years, reaching 12,262 metres in 1989 after 24 years of drilling. It was
not a mine but a scientific research project, funded by the Soviet Academy of
Sciences to study the deep structure of the Earth's crust. Scientific (research)
commodity; drilling halted 1992, facility closed 2008. The infrastructure is
derelict; the borehole itself has been welded shut. The site is near Zapolyarny
in the Kola Peninsula and is accessible to determined visitors, though there
is little to see beyond the rusting drill complex and a small monument.

## 5. Berezniki Potash Mines, Perm Krai

Berezniki is Russia's main potash and soda-ash producing city, built on top of
the world's second largest potash deposit. Potash commodity; operated by
Uralkali, continuously active. The mines have been plagued by sinkholes
resulting from dissolution of salt layers by infiltrating water: the Berezniki-1
sinkhole, which opened in 2006 and expanded continuously, swallowed several
city blocks. Additional sinkholes in 2007 and 2010 further destabilised the
city. Parts of Berezniki are being methodically relocated as subsidence threatens
housing and infrastructure.

## 6. Aikhal Diamond Mine, Yakutia

Aikhal (Jubilee) is one of Russia's largest diamond kimberlite pipes, operated
by Alrosa (a state-majority company), active since 1986. Diamond commodity;
active open-pit and underground operations, no public access. Alrosa controls
approximately 90 percent of Russian diamond production and is one of the two
or three largest diamond producers in the world. Aikhal is reached by air from
Mirny.

## 7. Mayak Nuclear Facility, Chelyabinsk Oblast

Mayak (Production Association Mayak) was the Soviet Union's primary nuclear
material production facility. Its 1957 Kyshtym explosion — a chemical explosion
of improperly stored high-level radioactive waste — was the third worst nuclear
accident in history. It contaminated over 20,000 square kilometres and resulted
in the secret forced evacuation of around 10,000 people. The facility remains
active and is a closed zone with no public access. The East Ural Radioactive
Trace — the contamination plume — is mapped but restricted.

## 8. Apatity, Murmansk Oblast

Apatity is built around the Khibiny Massif apatite-nepheline deposits, one of
the world's largest phosphate resources. Apatite commodity; operated by
PhosAgro, continuously active. The Khibiny Mountains contain the largest
apatite deposits in the world and produce phosphate fertiliser for global
agriculture. The Geological Museum of the Kola Science Centre in Apatity holds
one of the finest mineral collections from Arctic Russia.

## 9. Kuzbass Coal Heritage, Kemerovo Oblast

The Kuznetsk Basin contains coal museums at Prokopyevsk, Kemerovo, and
Novokuznetsk, covering the Soviet-era industrial history of the region. Coal
commodity; active coalfield, heritage museums accessible. The 2021
Listvyazhnaya mine disaster (51 dead), following the 2010 Raspadskaya disaster
(66 dead), demonstrated the ongoing operational risk in the Kuzbass. The 1989
Kuzbass miners' strikes — spontaneous walkouts that spread across Soviet
industry — are memorialised in workers' histories at several mines.

## 10. Yubileyny (Jubilee) Diamond Pipe, Yakutia

Yubileyny is among the largest diamond open-pit mines in the world by ore body
volume. Diamond commodity; operated by Alrosa, active. The open pit is visible
from air and satellite; the adjacent underground development is ongoing.
Alrosa's operations in Yakutia were subject to international sanctions reviews
following 2022 events, complicating diamond supply chain traceability.

## Russia's Mining Heritage and Access

Most significant Russian mining sites are either active (and therefore
restricted) or in remote areas requiring special permits. The Kuzbass coal
museums are the most readily accessible heritage sites for independent visitors.
The Kola Superdeep Borehole site is technically reachable but offers little
beyond the story itself. Sites that are accessible are on the [interactive map](/map);
sites that require permits or are in closed zones are noted accordingly.

## The Gulag and Mining Geography

Soviet mining is inseparable from the Gulag system. The NKVD's Chief
Administration of Corrective Labour Camps operated mining projects across
Siberia, the Kola Peninsula, Central Asia, and the Soviet Far East from the
1930s to the mid-1950s. Major mining operations built on Gulag labour include
Norilsk (nickel-copper), Magadan (gold — the Kolyma goldfields), Vorkuta
(coal, northern Komi Republic), and Solikamsk (potash). The Magadan Oblast,
in the Russian Far East, was the transit point for hundreds of thousands of
prisoners transported to the Kolyma goldfields; the Mask of Sorrow memorial
above Magadan, designed by Ernst Neizvestny and unveiled in 1996, is the
most significant public monument to Gulag victims in Russia.

The gold of Kolyma — produced by prisoners working in conditions of starvation,
cold, and violence between 1932 and 1953 — contributed significantly to the
Soviet gold reserve. The Kolyma Highway, built by Gulag labour, connects
Magadan to Yakutsk through 2,000 kilometres of permafrost and is still an
active road.  Varlam Shalamov's Kolyma Tales (written 1954–1973, first
published abroad 1978) remains the defining literary account of Gulag mining
labour. The International Memorial organisation, which documented Gulag
geography until its forced liquidation by the Russian authorities in 2021,
produced the most comprehensive map of Gulag camp locations, now maintained
by international partners.
