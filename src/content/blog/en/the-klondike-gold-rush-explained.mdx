In August 1896 three prospectors found gold on Rabbit Creek (renamed Bonanza) in the Yukon. Within two years 100 000 people had set out for the Klondike — perhaps the most photographed gold rush in history.

## Discovery

George Carmack, Skookum Jim Mason and Tagish Charley made the strike on Rabbit Creek, a tributary of the Klondike River, on 16 August 1896. News reached the outside world only the following summer.

## The stampede

When the steamships Excelsior and Portland docked in San Francisco and Seattle in July 1897 with two tons of Klondike gold, panic followed. Perhaps 100 000 people set off; only around a third made it to Dawson City.

## The Chilkoot and White Pass trails

Most stampeders came up the Inside Passage to Skagway in Alaska, then over the Chilkoot or White Pass into Canadian territory. The Canadian government required each entrant to carry one tonne of provisions — a ton of supplies — to prevent starvation.

## Dawson City

Dawson grew from nothing to 30 000 inhabitants by 1898 — briefly the largest city north of San Francisco and west of Winnipeg. The wooden frontier town built saloons, theatres and newspapers within a single winter.

## Mining methods

Klondike gold was largely placer — recoverable from frozen river gravels. Stampeders thawed the permafrost with fires and lifted gravel from shafts. Industrial dredging (1899–1966) eventually moved more gravel than all the stampeders combined.

## Indigenous impact

The Han people of the Klondike were displaced from their traditional fishing grounds. Chief Isaac of the Tr'ondëk Hwëch'in led the community's relocation and is commemorated in Dawson today.

## End of the rush

The discovery of gold at Nome, Alaska, in 1899 drew thousands away from the Klondike. Dawson's population fell to 5 000 by 1902 and to 1 000 by 1920. The town survived only as a service centre for industrial dredging.

## Heritage

Klondike Gold Rush National Historical Park (joint US-Canada inscription includes Skagway and Chilkoot Pass). Parks Canada manages Dawson and the Bonanza Creek dredging fields. The narrow-gauge White Pass and Yukon Route railway runs as a tourist line.

## Cultural legacy

Jack London, Robert Service and Pierre Berton each made the Klondike a permanent fixture of North American literature. The 1898 Dawson Daily News archive is one of the most complete surviving boomtown records anywhere.
