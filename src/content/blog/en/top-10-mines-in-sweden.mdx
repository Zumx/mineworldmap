Sweden's mining tradition extends from the medieval copper and silver mines of
Bergslagen through the iron ore fields of Norrbotten that helped arm both sides
of the First World War. The country produced the world's first systematic
mining law (Bergordnung, 1649), the world's first industrial safety regulations
for mines, and the global metallurgical science that made the modern metals
industry possible. It also produces some of the most interesting show mines
in Europe.

## 1. Falun Copper Mine, Dalarna (UNESCO)

Stora Kopparberget (Great Copper Mountain) at Falun is one of the world's most
important industrial heritage sites. Copper commodity; active from at least the
9th century to 1992, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2001. At
peak production in the seventeenth century, Falun provided two-thirds of the
world's copper and its revenues financed Sweden's Baltic empire. The mine is
entered today via the Great Pit (Stora Stöten) — a collapse crater 100 metres
deep and 340 metres wide formed by a catastrophic subsidence in 1687 — and
underground guided tours extend into original 17th-century workings. The
associated mining city of Falun retains its coloured timber buildings; the
distinctive red oxide paint used throughout Sweden (Falun rödfärg) is a
by-product of the mine's pyrite processing.

## 2. Kiruna Iron Ore Mine, Norrbotten

Kiruna, in the Arctic north of Sweden, hosts the world's largest underground
iron ore mine. Iron commodity; operated by LKAB (Luossavaara-Kiirunavaara AB),
continuously active since 1898. The mine has created a subsidence zone that
has required the relocation of most of the city of Kiruna — approximately 6,000
residents and thousands of structures, including the historic kirche (city hall,
voted Sweden's most beautiful building and physically moved) — to a new site
3 kilometres east. The relocation, scheduled for completion around 2035, is the
largest planned urban relocation in modern European history. Underground tours
are available to the public.

## 3. Sala Silver Mine, Västmanland

Sala Silvergruva has been worked continuously for silver and lead from 1510 to
1962 — one of the longest documented working histories of any European mine.
Silver-lead commodity; closed 1962, now a museum with underground tours. The
mine descends to 155 metres and maintains a constant temperature of 2°C; it
offers overnight stays in an underground hotel room (the world's coldest hotel
room, at 2°C with warming sleeping equipment provided). The surface buildings
include seventeenth-century managers' residences and mining offices.

## 4. Långban, Värmland

Långban is perhaps the world's most celebrated single locality for mineralogical
specimens. Iron-manganese commodity; worked from the 16th century to 1972.
The Långban deposit has produced over 80 mineral species first described from
this locality — type localities of hauyne, richterite, manganoan diopside, and
dozens of others — including specimens of extraordinary aesthetic quality. The
Långban Mineralmuseum in the former mine manager's house holds a significant
collection and is open during summer.

## 5. Garpenberg, Dalarna

Garpenberg is one of the oldest continuously worked metal mines in the world,
with silver and base-metal production documented from 1299. Silver-zinc-lead-
copper commodity; operated today by Boliden AB, active to depths exceeding
1,400 metres. A heritage surface trail through the Garpenberg Gammelgruva
(old mine area) documents the early modern workings. The current underground
operation is one of the most productive silver mines in Europe.

## 6. Aitik Copper Mine, Norrbotten

Aitik near Gällivare is the largest open-pit copper mine in Europe and one of
the largest in the world. Copper-gold-silver commodity; operated by Boliden,
active since 1968. Surface views from the designated overlook; no underground
access. At approximately 550 metres deep and 3 kilometres long, Aitik produces
around 40 million tonnes of ore per year. It is one of the lowest-grade large
copper mines in operation, economically viable because of its enormous scale.

## 7. Pajala Iron, Norrbotten

The Kaunisvaara iron ore deposit near Pajala was developed by Northland Resources
from 2010 to 2014 when the company entered bankruptcy, having built a
processing plant and begun production before collapsing. Iron commodity;
development 2010–2014, idled. The story of Kaunisvaara is taught in mining
finance circles as a case study in the risks of junior developer iron ore
projects in commodity downturns.

## 8. Norberg Iron and Historical Mining District, Västmanland

Norberg in Bergslagen was a centre of charcoal iron smelting from medieval
times through the nineteenth century. Iron commodity; principal production
ended nineteenth century, heritage district preserved. The Norberg Mining Area
is a candidate on the Swedish UNESCO Tentative List as part of the broader
Bergslagen industrial landscape nomination. The preserved blast furnace at
Ängelsberg, within the Bergslagen region, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site (1993).

## 9. Boliden Gold Mine (historic), Västerbotten

The Boliden gold deposit in Västerbotten was discovered in 1924 and gave its
name to the Boliden company now operating across northern Europe. Gold-arsenic
commodity; worked 1926–1967. The Boliden Area Heritage Site in Skellefteå
municipality includes the original smelter (now a museum) and the drainage
system. Boliden's arsenic production created significant contamination at the
Kristineberg mine and at the Rönnskär smelter on the coast.

## 10. Stripa Iron and Research Mine, Örebro

The Stripa mine in Bergslagen was an iron mine converted in the 1970s and 1980s
to a major international research programme for radioactive waste repository
concepts — the Stripa Project, funded by Sweden, the USA, Canada, Japan, and
the OECD NEA. Iron-research commodity; iron production ended 1977, research
programme 1976–1992. The research demonstrated the hydrogeological feasibility
of granitic rock repositories and contributed directly to modern nuclear waste
disposal science.

## Planning a Swedish Mining Route

Falun and Garpenberg are both in Dalarna, two to three hours from Stockholm, and
combine naturally. The Bergslagen region between Falun and Örebro contains
a dozen heritage sites within a day's drive. Kiruna requires a flight or an
overnight train from Stockholm; allow two days for the mine tour and the city
relocation context. All Swedish sites are on the [interactive map](/map).

## Swedish Mining Science

Sweden's contribution to mineralogy and mining science is disproportionate to
its size. The Swedish chemist Carl Wilhelm Scheele identified manganese, barium,
molybdenum, tungsten, and chlorine, most from minerals collected in the
Bergslagen district. Axel Fredrik Cronstedt discovered nickel in 1751 from the
mineral niccolite at Garphyttan, Bergslagen. Johan Gottlieb Gahn isolated
manganese from pyrolusite in 1774. The Uppsala and Stockholm universities
maintained mineralogy collections closely linked to the mining industry through
the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. The Swedish Museum of Natural History
in Stockholm holds one of the finest Scandinavian mineral collections, much
of it derived from the Bergslagen mines.

The Swedish Bergmästare (mine master) system, which assigned regional government
oversight of all mining operations from the seventeenth century onward,
created an unusual combination of state regulation and private enterprise that
preserved ore body data and mine records in ways that made the later development
of Swedish economic geology particularly systematic. The Geological Survey of
Sweden (SGU), founded in 1858, is one of the oldest national geological surveys
in the world and its archives are a significant resource for mine heritage
researchers.

## Bergslagen as Heritage Region

The Bergslagen region of central Sweden — covering roughly the area between
Falun, Örebro, and Västerås — is the most historically significant mining
district in Scandinavia. The UNESCO Engelsberg Ironworks (1993) within
Bergslagen preserves the most complete surviving early industrial blast furnace
complex in the world. The region as a whole, including Falun, the Ängelsberg
ironworks, and multiple smaller heritage sites, functions as an open-air
museum of the pre-industrial metals economy that sustained Sweden for five
centuries.
