Silver underwrote medieval economies and built royal cities. These tours descend into the actual galleries — most still cold, wet and unforgettable.

## Kongsberg, Norway

A 2.3 km train ride into the King's Mine — once the largest source of silver in Europe.

## Schwaz, Tyrol, Austria

An 800-metre train ride into the 16th-century Sigmund Erbstollen — Europe's largest silver mine in its day.

## Banská Štiavnica, Slovakia (UNESCO)

Walk the Bartolomej and Glanzenberg adits in central Europe's richest silver town.

## Freiberg, Saxony, Germany

The Reiche Zeche, attached to the world's oldest mining academy (1765).

## Kutná Hora, Czech Republic (UNESCO)

Descend the Hrádek shaft into a town that once provided a third of Europe's silver.

## Potosí, Bolivia (UNESCO)

Working silver-and-tin galleries of Cerro Rico, the mountain that financed the Spanish empire — visits run by miners' co-ops.

## Guanajuato, Mexico (UNESCO)

The 18th-century Boca del Inferno shaft and the surface workings of La Valenciana.

## Tellure, Sainte-Marie-aux-Mines, France

Alsatian silver galleries from the 1500s, accessed by a small train.

## Pribram, Czech Republic

The Vojtěch shaft — first in the world to reach a kilometre depth.

## Hiendelaencina, Spain

A 19th-century Castilian silver field with a small museum and short visitor adit.

## How to plan a tour

Bring a jumper — almost every silver gallery sits at 8–12 °C year-round. Most sites have visitor centres open from spring to autumn and offer guided descents in English in summer.
