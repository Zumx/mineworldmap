Bolivia's mining history is inseparable from its colonial history and its
continued poverty. Cerro Rico de Potosí may be the most consequential single
mountain in the history of capitalism: the silver it produced from 1545 onward
bankrolled the Spanish empire and funded a circulation of metal that transformed
global trade. The mines that followed in its wake have continued to extract
tin, zinc, gold, and silver under conditions that remain dangerous. Visiting
Bolivian mines requires both curiosity and a willingness to confront what
extraction has cost.

## 1. Cerro Rico, Potosí (UNESCO)

The mountain that ate men. Cerro Rico (Rich Hill) above Potosí has been mined
continuously since 1545 when Diego Huallpa found silver ore near the summit.
Silver commodity; active to the present day. At peak colonial production in the
late sixteenth century, Potosí was one of the largest cities in the world. An
estimated eight million enslaved and coerced indigenous workers — under the
mita forced-labour system — died in the mines and processing works over the
colonial period. The city of Potosí was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage
Site in 1987. The mines are still worked by cooperative miners (cooperativistas)
using manual methods largely unchanged from the colonial era. Visitor tours,
operated from Potosí, descend into active workings. The miners use dynamite
purchased at the miners' market; tourists often buy offerings of coca leaf,
cigarettes, and alcohol as gifts for the El Tío shrine.

## 2. El Tío Shrine, Cerro Rico

El Tío (the Uncle) is the deity of the mine — a horned, red-faced figure
installed at the entrance to underground workings throughout Potosí and beyond.
He is a syncretic figure, combining pre-Columbian earth-deity traditions with
colonial-era demonology. Miners make offerings of coca, tobacco, alcohol, and
llama fat to El Tío before entering the mine and on Fridays (the ch'alla
ceremony), in exchange for protection and ore. The figure appears in dozens
of mines; each working team maintains their own. Understanding El Tío is
essential to understanding the spiritual and cultural context of Bolivian
mining.

## 3. Pulacayo Mine, Potosí Department

Pulacayo was one of the most productive silver and tin mines in Bolivia during
the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. Silver-tin commodity; closed 1950s.
The mine railway at Pulacayo includes the Esmeralda locomotive, reportedly used
by Butch Cassidy and the Sundance Kid as a getaway vehicle after a 1908 robbery
— whether this is historical or promotional is debated. The ghost town of
Pulacayo retains its engine shed, processing plant ruins, and miners' housing,
and was named a UNESCO Tentative List candidate in 2000.

## 4. San Cristóbal Mine, Potosí Department

San Cristóbal is one of the largest silver-zinc-lead mines in the world, located
at 4,100 metres elevation on the altiplano. Silver-zinc-lead commodity; operated
by Sumitomo Corporation since 2007. Open-pit operation; no public access.
The project required the relocation of the entire village of San Cristóbal —
500 residents, their church, and their cemetery — before mining could begin.
The mine produces around 18 million ounces of silver annually.

## 5. Llallagua and Catavi, Potosí Department

Llallagua-Catavi was the centre of Bolivian tin production under Simón Patiño,
who became one of the wealthiest men in the world on the back of tin mining.
Tin commodity; peak production 1900s–1940s. The Catavi Massacre of December 1942
— when Bolivian army troops fired on striking miners and their families,
killing an uncertain number — became one of the defining events of Bolivian
labour history and contributed to the 1952 revolution. The Siglo XX mine at
Llallagua has a heritage museum and is still partially worked by cooperatives.

## 6. Huanuni Tin Mine, Oruro Department

Huanuni is one of the few remaining large-scale tin mines in Bolivia, operated
by COMIBOL (the state mining company) since its renationalisation under the
Morales government. Tin commodity; continuously active. The mine was the site
of a violent inter-cooperative conflict in 2006 that killed 16 miners. It
remains an important operational context for understanding Bolivia's ongoing
mining politics.

## 7. Coro Coro Copper Mine, La Paz Department

Coro Coro has been worked for copper since at least 1600 and was one of the
most important copper producers in South America during the colonial period.
Native copper occurred in veins within sandstone — unusual for the continent.
Copper commodity; largely inactive since mid-twentieth century. The town of
Coro Coro retains colonial-era processing ruins.

## 8. San Vicente Mine, Potosí Department

San Vicente is noted for producing some of Bolivia's finest silver specimens,
particularly acanthite (silver sulphide). It is also associated with the
supposed deaths of Butch Cassidy and the Sundance Kid in November 1908,
following a payroll robbery. Silver commodity; worked intermittently, currently
under exploration by junior mining companies. No confirmed public tours.

## 9. Apolo Gold District, La Paz Department

The Apolo district in La Paz Department has been worked for alluvial and
lode gold since colonial times. Gold commodity; still actively explored and
worked by artisanal and small-scale miners. The area is remote, in the transition
zone between the altiplano and the lowland yungas. No formal heritage tourism
infrastructure exists, but the district is geologically significant and
increasingly explored by junior mining companies including Orvana Minerals
(San Antonio mine area).

## 10. Quetena Chico, Potosí Department

The Quetena area on the border of the Eduardo Avaroa Andean Fauna National
Reserve contains copper-gold mineralisation worked at small scale since the
colonial period. Copper-gold commodity; artisanal operations ongoing. The area
is more commonly visited for the Laguna Colorada and Laguna Verde landscapes,
but the mine workings visible along the salt flat margins are historically
continuous from early colonial activity.

## Visiting Bolivia's Mines

Any visit to Cerro Rico should be arranged through a Potosí-based guide, not
done independently. The mines are active, the workings are genuinely dangerous,
and entering without a guide and helmet is illegal as well as reckless. The
standard tour involves buying offerings at the miners' market, visiting the El
Tío shrine, and observing (and potentially assisting with) active drilling or
ore loading. It is physically demanding at altitude. All Bolivian mining sites
in this article are plotted on the [interactive map](/map), where you can filter
by commodity and access status to distinguish between active heritage tours and

## The Human Cost

Bolivia remains the only country where active mine tourism routinely brings
visitors into working faces alongside miners using hand drills in conditions
that would be illegal in any OECD country. Life expectancy for Potosí cooperative
miners who begin before age 20 averages around 40 years, due to silicosis and
accidents. This is not an abstraction. Visitors who take the Cerro Rico tour
should understand that the mine is not a museum and the conditions are not
theatrical.

The cooperative structure of Bolivian mining — established after the 1952
revolution and entrenched after the 1985 collapse of state tin mining under
the Paz Estenssoro structural adjustment — leaves miners as technically
self-employed workers with no employer-provided safety equipment, no
occupational health coverage, and no pension beyond what the cooperative
chooses to provide. Child labour in Bolivian artisanal mining, documented
repeatedly by Human Rights Watch and UNICEF, is a direct consequence of this
system's poverty dynamics. The Defensoría del Pueblo (Ombudsman) of Bolivia
has repeatedly called for reform of working conditions in Cerro Rico and other
cooperative mines. Understanding this context does not make the El Tío shrine
less fascinating or the underground landscape less extraordinary — but it
changes what a visitor is there to witness.

All Bolivian sites are on the [interactive map](/map). Cerro Rico tours are
arranged exclusively through licensed Potosí guides; independent entry to the
working mines is prohibited. For the UNESCO historic city of Potosí itself,
the Museo de la Moneda (the colonial mint) is one of the finest museum
experiences in South America and provides essential context for the silver
economy that Cerro Rico sustained.
