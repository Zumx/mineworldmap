Mexico was, for most of the colonial period, the world's largest silver producer.
The great silver-mining cities — Zacatecas, Guanajuato, Taxco, Pachuca — are
among the finest colonial urban landscapes in the Americas. The mines beneath
them are still accessible, whether as heritage tours, active operations, or
semi-preserved ghost towns. Silver is still Mexico's most important precious
metal by historical weight, though gold and copper now dominate by revenue.

## 1. Guanajuato, Guanajuato (UNESCO)

The colonial city of Guanajuato and its surrounding mines were inscribed as a
UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1988. The Valenciana mine, opened in 1766,
became one of the most productive silver mines in the world at the height of
New Spain and funded the extraordinary Churrigueresque church of La Valenciana
above its shaft. Silver commodity; Valenciana active in limited production to
the present under Grupo Industrial Minera Mexico. The Boca del Inferno (Mouth
of Hell) shaft at Valenciana is accessible to visitors and conveys the depth
and scale of colonial mine engineering. The underground canal system running
beneath the city streets adds to a mining geography that is literally built
into the urban fabric.

## 2. Zacatecas, Zacatecas (UNESCO)

The city of Zacatecas was founded in 1546 when silver was discovered on the
Cerro de la Bufa, and its historic centre was inscribed as a UNESCO World
Heritage Site in 1993. The Mina El Edén operated commercially until the 1960s
and is now the city's most visited attraction — visitors descend by cable car
or elevator and tour galleries on electric train, then exit into a nightclub
operated inside the former mine workings. Silver commodity; closed 1960s,
heritage and cultural use ongoing. The mine produced silver and zinc over four
centuries; the museum displays include colonial-era smelting equipment.

## 3. Pachuca, Hidalgo

Pachuca is known as the "Real del Monte de Plata" and was among the most
productive silver districts in New Spain. A significant influx of Cornish
miners in the 1820s and 1830s brought British capital and steam technology;
their descendants formed communities that introduced football (soccer) and
pasties to Mexico. Silver commodity; some active operations continue under
Grupo Peñoles. The Museo de Minería in Pachuca covers the pre-Columbian,
colonial, and modern eras, and the Real del Monte mining town 13 kilometres
uphill preserves its Cornish-influenced architecture and cemeteries.

## 4. Real de Catorce, San Luis Potosí

Real de Catorce was a silver boom town in the late eighteenth and nineteenth
centuries, producing enormous quantities of silver from the 1770s to the 1910s.
Silver commodity; principal production ended with the Mexican Revolution, ghost
town from 1910 onward. The town — population once around 40,000, now around
1,000 — is reached through a 2.3-kilometre cobblestone tunnel through the
mountain. It retains its mint, churches, and theatre in varying states of
preservation. The area has become a significant destination for Wixáritari
(Huichol) people for their peyote pilgrimage, adding a living Indigenous
cultural dimension to the site.

## 5. Taxco, Guerrero

Taxco de Alarcón was the site of the first major silver mine in the Americas
after the Spanish conquest, with production beginning around 1534. The city's
extraordinary Baroque Iglesia de Santa Prisca (1751–1758) was built by silver
baron José de la Borda at extraordinary personal cost. Silver commodity;
artisan silver working continues commercially. Taxco is not primarily a mine
heritage destination but a silver craft city — the heritage of the mine is now
expressed through hundreds of silver workshops.

## 6. Mineral del Pozos, Guanajuato

El Mineral del Pozos (also known as Pozos) was a prosperous silver and copper
mining town in the nineteenth century, largely abandoned after 1910. Silver-
copper commodity; ghost town, limited boutique tourist repopulation. The ruins
of nineteenth-century haciendas, the Cinco Señores mine headframe, and the
roofless church are now the setting for artists' studios and a boutique hotel
scene. It is one of the most atmospheric mining ghost towns in Mexico.

## 7. Cananea, Sonora

Cananea is famous in Mexican labour history as the site of the 1906 Cananea
strike, in which Mexican copper miners struck against the American-owned Cananea
Consolidated Copper Company and its discriminatory wage practices, triggering
a violent suppression involving Arizona Rangers crossing the border. Copper
commodity; operated today by Grupo Mexico (Buenavista del Cobre mine, one of
the world's largest copper producers). No heritage visitor access to active
operations. The 1906 strike is memorialised in Cananea town.

## 8. Fresnillo, Zacatecas

Fresnillo has been worked for silver since the sixteenth century and is now
operated by Fresnillo plc — listed on the London Stock Exchange and the world's
largest primary silver producer. Silver commodity; active underground mining.
No public access. The mine's continuous history from colonial to contemporary
operations is rare and makes it an important reference point for understanding
the persistence of silver in Mexico's economic geography.

## 9. El Boleo, Santa Rosalía, Baja California Sur

El Boleo was a French-developed copper mine operated by the Compagnie du Boleo
from 1885 to 1954, making Santa Rosalía a French company town in the desert.
Copper commodity; relaunched as Baja Mining Corp (later Korea Resources
Corporation involvement) in 2012, subsequently suspended. The town retains its
distinctive wood-frame French colonial architecture, a prefabricated church
reportedly designed by Gustave Eiffel, and an atmospheric industrial landscape.

## 10. San Felipe el Real de Chihuahua, Chihuahua

San Felipe el Real (colonial name for the city of Chihuahua) was founded in
1709 as a silver mining town. The Cathedral of Chihuahua, built between 1717
and 1826, was funded by a voluntary one-percent silver tithe from local miners.
Silver-gold commodity; various active exploration projects in surrounding area.
The Museo Casa Chihuahua in the former government palace and the Centro Cultural
Universitario provide historical context for the northern Mexico silver tradition.

## Planning a Mexican Silver Trail

Mexico's UNESCO silver cities — Guanajuato, Zacatecas — are both accessible
by bus from Mexico City, roughly four and five hours respectively, and work
well as a combined route through the Bajío highland. Real de Catorce requires
a longer commitment: overnight in Matehuala, then a jeep through the tunnel.
Taxco is south of Mexico City, two to three hours. All Mexican mining sites

## Silver and Mexican Independence

The silver mines of New Spain were the economic engine of the colonial order
and, paradoxically, contributed to its overthrow. Miguel Hidalgo's Grito de
Dolores in September 1810, which launched the independence war, took place
in the Bajío — the same agricultural region whose population had been
disrupted by the labour demands and economic distortions of Guanajuato and
Zacatecas silver production. The Alhóndiga de Granaditas in Guanajuato, a
grain warehouse that was the site of the first major insurgent military
action in September 1810 — where miners and workers stormed the royalist
garrison using a large flat stone as a battering ram, in the famous episode
of El Pípila — is now the Museo Regional de Guanajuato. The hooks from
which the severed heads of Hidalgo, Allende, Aldama, and Jiménez were
suspended as a warning after their capture in 1811 are still visible at
the building's four corners.

The 1810–1821 independence war disrupted silver production severely; the
mining districts of Guanajuato and Zacatecas were contested militarily and
many mines flooded when pumping operations were abandoned. The reconstruction
of the industry after 1821, with significant British capital (the Real del
Monte company, Cornish miners, and the Cornish engine houses visible in
Pachuca today), was one of the formative episodes of Mexican economic
history.
