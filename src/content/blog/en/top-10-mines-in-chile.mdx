Chile is the world's largest copper producer, accounting for around a quarter
of global output. Its mining history spans Atacama nitrate extraction — which
fertilised nineteenth-century European agriculture and fuelled the arms race
before synthetic nitrogen ended the industry — through the great twentieth-
century copper nationalisation under Allende, to the contemporary lithium
debate. The mines here are monumental in scale and freighted with political
history.

## 1. Chuquicamata, Antofagasta Region

One of the largest open-pit copper mines in the world, 4.3 kilometres long,
3 kilometres wide, and 850 metres deep. Copper commodity; operated by Codelco
(the Chilean state copper company) since 1971. The town of Chuquicamata was
relocated in 2007 when subsidence threatened to make it uninhabitable;
approximately 18,000 residents were moved to Calama. A public viewing platform
on the pit rim allows visitors to grasp the scale. The mine has produced copper
continuously since 1910, initially under Anaconda Copper (Guggenheim interests).

## 2. El Teniente, O'Higgins Region

The largest underground copper mine in the world by tunnel length, with more
than 3,000 kilometres of tunnels extending into the Andes mountains at around
2,000 metres elevation. Copper commodity; operated by Codelco, active. The
El Cobre tourism programme offers guided underground and surface tours from
the nearby town of Rancagua, making El Teniente one of the few large active
underground copper mines in the world with regular visitor access. The mine
has produced copper since 1905 under Braden Copper Company (later Kennecott).

## 3. Sewell Ghost Town, O'Higgins Region (UNESCO)

Sewell was the company town built by Braden Copper to house El Teniente workers
at 2,000 metres altitude — a city of 15,000 people at its peak with no road
access, only a funicular railway. Copper commodity; abandoned 1970s. Inscribed
as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2006, Sewell is reached by a heritage train
from Rancagua. The multi-coloured buildings, the staircase urbanism forced by
the steep terrain, and the preservation of a fully intact company town make
it one of the finest industrial heritage sites in South America.

## 4. Humberstone and Santa Laura Nitrate Works (UNESCO)

The nitrate oficinas of the Atacama are the ruins of an industry that lasted
roughly 1880–1930 and then collapsed virtually overnight when the Haber-Bosch
process made synthetic nitrogen available. Nitrate (saltpetre) commodity;
Humberstone and Santa Laura inscribed as UNESCO World Heritage Sites in 2005.
Both are accessible on the Panamericana Norte north of Iquique, within walking
distance of each other. Humberstone retains its teatro, church, swimming pool,
hotel, and processing plant in a state of spectacular atmospheric decay.

## 5. Chacabuco, Antofagasta Region

Chacabuco is a nitrate oficina that served a double historical purpose: first
as a working town of 7,000 people during the nitrate era, then as a concentration
camp for opponents of the Pinochet coup from 1973 to 1975. Nitrate commodity;
closed 1940, repurposed as detention facility 1973–1975. Now a monument to
both industrial heritage and human rights. Visitors can walk the entire townsite;
the cemetery contains the graves of nitrate workers from the late nineteenth century.

## 6. María Elena, Antofagasta Region

María Elena is the last surviving working nitrate town in Chile — the only
oficina that continued production into the twenty-first century. Operated by
SQM (Sociedad Química y Minera de Chile), it extracts iodine and nitrate from
caliche ore. Nitrate-iodine commodity; active since 1926. The town retains its
company-town grid layout, theatre, and residential streets and was declared a
National Monument in 2000.

## 7. Salar de Atacama Lithium, Antofagasta Region

The Salar de Atacama is the world's most productive source of lithium, extracted
from underground brine by SQM and Albemarle Corporation. Lithium-potassium
commodity; active and expanding. No public access to the processing ponds.
The Salar sits within the ancestral territory of the Atacameño (Lickanantay)
people, and brine extraction's impact on the freshwater aquifers that feed
the oasis communities of the Atacama has been the subject of prolonged legal
and regulatory dispute.

## 8. Mantos Blancos, Antofagasta Region

Mantos Blancos is a copper mine in the Atacama coastal range operated by
Mantos Copper (majority owned by Japonese conglomerate Marubeni). Copper
commodity; active since 1961. No public access. The mine is significant as
an example of the mid-tier copper operations that constitute much of Chile's
copper industry outside the Codelco giants — a sector that employs tens of
thousands of Chileans.

## 9. Andina, Valparaíso Region

Codelco's Andina mine sits in the Andes east of Santiago at elevations between
3,500 and 4,500 metres, partly underground and partly open-cut. Copper commodity;
operated by Codelco, active. No regular public access. Andina is notable for
the ongoing Proyecto Minero Andina 244 expansion proposal, which has faced
significant opposition from communities in the Aconcagua River valley over
glacial water resource impacts.

## 10. Escondida, Antofagasta Region

The world's largest copper mine by production, operated by BHP (57.5 percent),
Rio Tinto (30 percent), and JECO (12.5 percent). Copper commodity; active since
1990. No public access. At peak production Escondida has accounted for around
5 percent of world copper supply. It operates at altitude on the Atacama
puna and uses desalinated sea water pumped 3,000 metres uphill — one of the
largest desalination and pumping operations in the mining world.

## Understanding Chilean Copper History

The Chilean copper industry was nationalised by the Allende government in 1971
under the Great Nationalisation (Gran Minería). Codelco, the state company,
continues to operate the major deposits while foreign companies (BHP, Rio Tinto,
Anglo American, Glencore) operate the private sector mines under long-term
concessions. The 2010 Copiapó mine rescue — 33 miners trapped underground for
69 days at the San José mine near Copiapó before being successfully extracted
— became one of the most widely watched industrial events in history. All
Chilean mining sites are on the [interactive map](/map), with UNESCO status and
visitor access indicated.

## The Atacama as Industrial Landscape

The Atacama Desert is the world's driest non-polar desert and simultaneously
one of its most intensively mined regions. The combination of extreme aridity
(which preserved organic artefacts in the nitrate towns for over a century
without deterioration) and mineral concentration (from ancient volcanic activity
and tectonic uplift) makes it unique. The UNESCO nitrate towns — Humberstone,
Santa Laura, and the Tentative List candidates — represent an industry that
rose and collapsed in 50 years, leaving behind entire cities of industrial
archaeology.

Visiting the Atacama mining landscape is best anchored in Iquique (for the
nitrate towns) or Calama (for Chuquicamata and the Atacama's eastern edge).
The drive between Iquique and Antofagasta on Ruta 1 passes dozens of ruined
oficinas and rail sidings; several are accessible on foot and most are
unlocked. The landscape itself is the exhibit. Bring sunscreen at altitude,
carry more water than you think you need, and note that temperatures drop
sharply after sunset at altitude even when the midday heat is extreme.

## Mine Safety in Chile's History

The 1945 Semita disaster at the El Teniente mine (355 dead from a fire caused
by burning underground timber) remains the worst mining accident in Chilean
history. The 2010 San José rescue, while ending without fatalities, exposed
the working conditions typical of small Chilean copper-gold mines operating
outside the Codelco and major multinational systems. Chilean mine safety law
was substantially revised after the San José accident, with mandatory electronic
tracking, improved emergency refuges, and enhanced inspection regimes.
