South Africa's mining industry is the foundation of its modern economy and
the source of its deepest inequalities. The gold and diamond rushes of the
1870s and 1880s drew capital and labour from across the world and were the
direct cause of the Anglo-Boer Wars. The migrant labour system that sustained
the mines destroyed family and community life across southern Africa. And yet
the minerals themselves — the Cullinan diamond, the Witwatersrand gold, the
Kimberley diamonds — are objects of extraordinary human significance.

## 1. Big Hole, Kimberley, Northern Cape

The Kimberley Mine, known as the Big Hole, is the largest hole excavated by
hand in the world: 214 metres deep, 463 metres wide, dug by some 50,000 workers
between 1871 and 1914. Diamond commodity; closed 1914. The Big Hole is
surrounded by the Kimberley Mine Museum, which reconstructs the 1880s mining
town with original buildings relocated to the site. A glass platform cantilevered
over the hole and a surface heritage precinct make this one of the finest
industrial heritage experiences in southern Africa.

## 2. Cullinan Diamond Mine, Gauteng

The Cullinan mine near Pretoria produced the Cullinan Diamond in 1905 — at
3,106 carats, the largest gem-quality rough diamond ever found. It was
subsequently cut into the Great Star of Africa (530.2 carats, now in the
Sovereign's Sceptre) and the Second Star of Africa (317.4 carats, now in the
Imperial State Crown). Diamond commodity; operated by Petra Diamonds since
2008 from De Beers, active underground to approximately 1,073 metres. Guided
underground tours available to the public. The mine is also known as the
Premier Mine.

## 3. Gold Reef City, Johannesburg, Gauteng

Gold Reef City is a heritage theme park and casino on the site of the former
Crown Mines, one of the most productive gold mines on the Witwatersrand.
Gold commodity; mining operations 1887–1976 at Crown, heritage park opened
1987. An underground tour descends to the 226-metre level in an original
shaft and shows gold-pouring demonstrations with authentic equipment. The
surface precinct reproduces the Edwardian mining camp with original buildings
relocated from the Witwatersrand.

## 4. Vredefort Dome, Free State and Gauteng (UNESCO)

The Vredefort Dome is the eroded remnant of the world's oldest and largest
verified meteorite impact structure, 2.02 billion years old, inscribed as a
UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2005. Its relevance to mining heritage is
fundamental: the impact created the geological conditions that concentrated the
Witwatersrand gold reefs. Gold commodity (indirect); the dome itself is a
geological and heritage tourism site south of Johannesburg. The relationship
between the impact structure and the gold deposits is explained at the
Vredefort Dome Visitor Centre in Parys.

## 5. TauTona Mine, Carletonville, Gauteng

TauTona was the deepest mine in the world at 3,900 metres from 2008 until
it was surpassed by Mponeng. Gold commodity; operated by AngloGold Ashanti,
primary production phased out by 2018. No public access. The mine's
infrastructure — the hoisting cables, the ventilation requirements, the
refrigeration needed to work at depths where rock temperatures exceed 60°C —
represents the extreme engineering that Witwatersrand deep mining demands.

## 6. Mponeng Mine, Carletonville, Gauteng

Mponeng is currently the deepest mine in the world, reaching approximately
4,000 metres at its deepest working levels. Gold commodity; operated by
AngloGold Ashanti, active. No public access. The mine employs around 4,000
workers who descend in multi-stage hoisting cycles taking over an hour to reach
the working faces. Rock at the deepest levels is cooled by ice slurry pumped
from surface.

## 7. Driefontein Mine, Carletonville, Gauteng

Driefontein (now Gold Fields South Deep combined operation) was among the
most productive gold mines in South Africa at peak. Gold commodity; operated
by Gold Fields, active underground. No public access. South Deep, at
2,995 metres, holds one of the largest remaining gold reserves in the world,
with an estimated 28 million ounces remaining in the ground.

## 8. Sterkfontein Caves, Maropeng, Gauteng (UNESCO)

The Sterkfontein Caves are part of the Cradle of Humankind UNESCO World
Heritage Site (1999). They are limestone caves worked for calcified bone by
fossil hunters from 1895 onward and have produced the largest collection of
hominin fossils from a single site in the world. Commodity: limestone (surface);
palaeoanthropological heritage (primary). Mrs Ples (Australopithecus africanus,
2 million years old) and Little Foot (3.67 million years old) were both found
here. Underground tours of the cave system run daily.

## 9. Pilgrim's Rest, Mpumalanga

Pilgrim's Rest was a gold rush alluvial field from 1873 and a company town
of the Transvaal Gold Mining Estates from 1896 to 1972. Gold commodity; closed
1972, entire town declared a National Monument. The Victorian-era townscape is
preserved largely intact — the church, the hotel, the miners' cottages — and
a surface mine tour operates at the Alanglade House Museum complex. It is the
only town in South Africa preserved as a national monument in its entirety.

## 10. Mapungubwe, Limpopo (UNESCO)

Mapungubwe on the Limpopo River was the centre of a southern African Iron Age
kingdom between approximately 900 and 1290 AD that traded gold northward to
the Swahili coast and into the Indian Ocean network. Gold commodity (prehistoric);
inscribed as UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2003. The golden rhinoceros and
other gold foil objects recovered from the royal burial site are displayed in
the Mapungubwe Collection at the University of Pretoria.

## Western Deep Levels / South Deep, Gauteng

The Western Deep Levels mines (now Kloof-Driefontein Complex under Gold Fields)
represent the extreme of Witwatersrand deep mining — operations at depths
approaching 4,000 metres with some of the highest seismic risk of any mine
in the world. Gold commodity; active. No public access. Seismicity from stress
changes in the rock causes rockbursts that have killed miners and driven
continuous investment in seismic monitoring and support systems.

## South Africa's Mining and its Human History

Any honest account of South African mining must acknowledge the migrant labour
system, the compound system, the Pass Laws, and the dispossession of mining
land. The 2012 Marikana massacre — 34 striking miners shot by police at a
Lonmin platinum mine — is the most recent of many instances of state violence
against mine workers. All heritage sites are on the [interactive map](/map);
the Big Hole and Cullinan are the most rewarding starting points for an
international visitor.

The compound system — which housed migrant workers in locked single-sex
hostels on or adjacent to mine properties — was developed on the Kimberley
diamond mines in the 1880s initially to prevent diamond smuggling. It was
subsequently adopted across the Witwatersrand gold mines and became the
institutional foundation of the migrant labour system. Workers were recruited
primarily from Mozambique, Lesotho, Malawi, Botswana, Zimbabwe, and the
South African Bantustans, with circular migration contracts of six to nine
months that prevented family accompaniment. The National Union of Mineworkers
(NUM), founded in 1982, was one of the most important organisations in the
anti-apartheid movement. The history of South African mining labour is told
most comprehensively at the MuseumAfrica in Johannesburg (formerly the Museum
of South African Rock Art), which holds extensive photographic and oral
history collections from the Chamber of Mines archives.

The Witwatersrand gold reefs are approaching their effective economic end.
South Deep's 28 million ounces of remaining reserve represent decades of
further production, but the depths involved — approaching 3,000 metres —
require continuous refrigeration, ground control, and mechanisation that make
each ounce progressively more costly. The era of deep Witwatersrand gold
mining, which lasted from 1886 to the present, is one of the defining
industrial episodes in world history.
