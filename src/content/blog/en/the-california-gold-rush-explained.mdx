James Marshall's discovery of gold at Sutter's Mill in January 1848 set off the largest mass migration in 19th-century American history. The Gold Rush reshaped California, the United States, and the Pacific.

## The discovery

Marshall pulled gold flakes from the tailrace of a sawmill he was building for John Sutter on the South Fork of the American River. Within months the news had spread to Mexico, Chile, Hawai'i and Europe.

## The Forty-Niners

By the end of 1849, around 300 000 people had reached California. Half came by sea via Cape Horn or the Panama isthmus; the other half overland by wagon train across the plains, mountains and deserts of the American West.

## Boomtowns

San Francisco grew from a settlement of 1000 in 1848 to 25 000 in 1850. Sacramento, Stockton and a string of Sierra Nevada mining towns multiplied in scale within months.

## Mining methods

Initial work was alluvial — panning, sluicing and 'long-toming' in the streambeds. From 1853 hydraulic mining became dominant, blasting hillsides with monitor guns. By the 1860s most production came from hard-rock quartz mining.

## The impact on Native Californians

The indigenous population of California fell from perhaps 150 000 in 1846 to 30 000 in 1870. Disease, violence and forced labour together amounted to what scholars now describe as genocide.

## Chinese miners

By 1860 there were 35 000 Chinese miners in California, many from the Pearl River delta. The Foreign Miners' Tax (1850, 1852) targeted them. The Chinese Exclusion Act (1882) was the eventual federal response.

## Statehood and politics

California's population growth forced statehood: it joined the Union in 1850 under the Compromise of 1850 as a free state, accelerating the sectional crisis that led to the Civil War.

## Environmental legacy

Hydraulic mining tore down entire ridges and choked downstream rivers with silt; the Sawyer Decision of 1884 banned the worst practices. Mercury used in amalgamation polluted the Sierra Nevada watercourses for over a century.

## Visiting the heritage

Marshall Gold Discovery State Historic Park at Coloma, Columbia State Historic Park, Sutter's Fort in Sacramento, and the string of towns along Highway 49 form one of the best-preserved mining-heritage routes anywhere.
