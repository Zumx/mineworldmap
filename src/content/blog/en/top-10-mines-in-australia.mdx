Australia's mining heritage runs from nineteenth-century gold rushes that
shaped the country's population and politics to some of the largest active
mines on earth. These ten sites represent the full range — gold, copper, opal,
uranium, lead, zinc, and the human stories buried with them.

## 1. Sovereign Hill, Ballarat, Victoria

The world's finest gold-rush living museum, set on an active goldfield that
produced 78 tonnes of gold between 1851 and 1861. Gold commodity; 1850s–1860s
peak period. Surface and reconstructed underground access. Sovereign Hill is
a working outdoor museum: visitors pan for gold in real tailings, descend
reconstructed underground drives, and watch period craft demonstrations. Still
independently operated and, remarkably, still finds commercial gold.

## 2. Super Pit, Kalgoorlie-Boulder, Western Australia

One of Australia's largest open-cut gold mines, 3.5 kilometres long and 570
metres deep. Gold commodity; active since 1989, currently operated by Northern
Star Resources. Surface access only at the public lookout on Outram Street.
The pit was formed by combining numerous underground mines that had worked the
Golden Mile reef since 1893. It operates continuously, blasting on a daily
schedule that visitors can check online.

## 3. Cobar Copper Mine, New South Wales

The Great Cobar copper and gold mine in western New South Wales has been worked
intermittently since 1871. Copper and gold commodity; currently operated by
Aurelia Metals. Underground and open-cut operations are active; the Cobar
Heritage Centre includes surface heritage displays. The town of Cobar was
purpose-built for the mine and retains significant Federation-era architecture
from the 1900s boom period.

## 4. Mount Isa, Queensland

One of the largest single-mine operations in the world, producing copper, lead,
zinc, and silver from the same ore body. Multi-commodity; operated by Glencore.
Active underground and open-cut operations. The Hard Times Mine Tour, run from
Mount Isa's Outback at Isa centre, provides underground access to inactive
workings for visitors. The ore body was discovered in 1923 and has been in
continuous production since 1931.

## 5. Olympic Dam, South Australia

The fourth-largest copper deposit in the world and the largest known uranium
deposit. Copper-uranium-gold-silver commodity; operated by BHP since 1988,
with underground mining infrastructure at depths reaching 900 metres. No
public underground access. Olympic Dam is central to current Australian and
global debates about uranium production. The processing operation in Roxby
Downs township is one of the largest underground copper operations on earth.

## 6. Coober Pedy, South Australia

The opal capital of the world, producing around 70 percent of the world's
gem-quality opal. Opal commodity; first discovered 1915, continuously worked
since. Unique in Australia for its underground town: dugout homes, churches,
and hotels are carved into the hillsides to escape extreme surface heat
(summer temperatures exceed 50°C). Multiple show mines operate, including the
Old Timers Mine and the Umoona Opal Mine and Museum.

## 7. White Cliffs, New South Wales

An early opal-producing region predating Coober Pedy; White Cliffs had opal
diggings from 1889 and was the first commercially worked opal field in
Australia. Opal commodity; largely ceased commercial production but boutique
fossicking continues. Several dugout dwellings and show workings are accessible
to visitors. The stark Jump-Up landscape with its hundreds of mullock heaps is
one of the most distinctive opal field terrains in Australia.

## 8. Mount Lyell, Queenstown, Tasmania

Copper mining on Mount Lyell transformed Queenstown and the King River valley
in ways that are still visible and contested. Copper commodity; operated since
1883, majority of operations wound down after 2014 though limited rehabilitation
works continue. The barren, multi-coloured hills around Queenstown — a result
of sulphur dioxide emissions killing all vegetation before the 1990s — are a
UNESCO nomination candidate for industrial heritage. The Galley Museum in
Queenstown covers the social history of the valley.

## 9. Broken Hill, New South Wales

Broken Hill is built on one of the world's great lead-zinc-silver ore bodies,
first worked in 1883 and operated continuously since. Lead-zinc-silver
commodity; currently operated by CBH Resources and Perilya. The Line of Lode
Miners Memorial on the ore body outcrop commemorates the 800 workers killed in
the mines over 140 years. The underground tour at Day Dream Mine, a preserved
Victorian-era silver mine nearby, provides historical context.

## 10. Wittenoom, Western Australia

Wittenoom was Australia's principal source of blue asbestos (crocidolite)
between 1943 and 1966. Asbestos commodity; permanently closed and officially
designated a contaminated site by the Western Australian government. Formerly
a town of 2,000 people, it is now officially a ghost town — it was removed from
road maps and de-gazetted in 2007. Approximately 140 former residents had died
of mesothelioma by the time the mine closed; the toll continues. Wittenoom is
not a tourist destination; it appears here as a reminder that mining heritage
includes the history of industrial poisoning.

## Understanding Australia's Mining Geography

Australia's mines cluster in three broad belts: the Eastern Goldfields of
Western Australia around Kalgoorlie, the South Australian copper-uranium arc
extending from Olympic Dam, and the eastern states multi-metal district
including Mount Isa and Broken Hill. Every mine listed here is on the
[interactive map](/map), where you can filter by state, commodity, and access
type to build a realistic itinerary. Distances between sites are large —
the country-scale map gives a true sense of this.

The Northern Territory adds a further dimension with its uranium history:
Ranger Uranium Mine in Kakadu closed in 2021 and rehabilitation is under
way; the nearby Jabiluka deposit is on care and maintenance despite Traditional
Owner opposition to development. Both sit within one of the most significant
Aboriginal cultural landscapes in the world. The relationship between resource
extraction and Indigenous land rights is nowhere in Australia more starkly
expressed.

## The Miners' Heritage Towns

Several of Australia's mining towns have survived the end of their primary
resource and developed strong heritage identities. Broken Hill established
itself as Australia's first Heritage City in 2015, recognising the extraordinary
concentration of heritage streetscape, artists, and history that a century
of silver-lead-zinc mining created. Charters Towers in Queensland, a gold
boom town from the 1870s that produced 6.6 million ounces before the boom
ended in 1917, retains its 1880s commercial streetscape largely intact and
is listed on the Queensland Heritage Register. Ravenswood, south of Townsville,
was Queensland's first major gold town and its pubs and stone buildings date
from the 1870s; gold production resumed at the Mount Wright mine in 1987
and continues today, giving the town an unusual continuity.

## Visiting Active vs Heritage Mines

Several mines in this list — Mount Isa, Olympic Dam, Super Pit — are active
industrial operations where public access is restricted to designated viewing
areas or ticketed heritage tours. Book ahead, check seasonal availability, and
confirm that active mines have not suspended tours. Heritage sites like
Sovereign Hill and Coober Pedy show mines operate year-round with no booking
required for standard entry. Underground temperatures in Australian show mines
range from the constant desert cool of Coober Pedy dugouts to the warm
humid deep levels of tropical Queensland mines — check conditions specific
to each site before packing.
