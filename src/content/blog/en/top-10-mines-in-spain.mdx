Spain was the most intensively mined territory of the Roman Empire: the Iberian
Peninsula's copper, gold, silver, and lead poured into the Roman economy at
a scale that shaped Mediterranean history. The physical evidence of that
extraction — the vast hydraulic scars of Las Médulas, the 2,000-year acid
rivers of Huelva — is still dramatically visible. Three Spanish mining sites
are on the UNESCO World Heritage List.

## 1. Río Tinto, Huelva, Andalusia

The Rio Tinto mines near Nerva in Huelva province are among the oldest
continuously worked mining sites in the world, with evidence of extraction
going back at least 5,000 years. Copper-gold-silver commodity; worked by
Tartessians, Phoenicians, Carthaginians, and Romans, then by British-owned
Rio Tinto Company from 1873 to 1954, and intermittently since. The Rio Tinto
itself — the "Red River" — runs orange-red with acid and iron hydroxide,
a direct consequence of acid mine drainage from 5,000 years of pyrite
exposure. NASA studies this river as an analogue environment for Mars's
ancient acidic aquatic history. The Museo Minero de Riotinto and the Parque
Minero offer surface tours, a mine railway, and an accessible section of the
Roman Corta del Lago open cut.

## 2. Almadén Mercury Mine, Ciudad Real (UNESCO)

Almadén in Castilla-La Mancha contains the world's largest known mercury
(cinnabar) deposit, worked continuously since Roman times. Mercury commodity;
operated by Minas de Almadén y Arrayanes, closed 2003, now a museum and
UNESCO World Heritage Site (2012, inscribed jointly with Idrija, Slovenia).
Mercury from Almadén was essential to New World silver processing via the
amalgamation method; the Spanish Crown's monopoly on Almadén mercury was
one of the most valuable economic privileges in early modern Europe. The
Parque Minero de Almadén includes an underground tour through eighteenth-
century workings and a museum of mercury-processing history.

## 3. Las Médulas Roman Gold Mine, Castilla y León (UNESCO)

Las Médulas in the Bierzo region of Castilla y León is the largest surviving
Roman hydraulic gold mine in the world. Gold commodity; operated by Rome using
the ruina montium (collapse of mountains) technique, approximately 1st to 3rd
century AD. Water from aqueducts was impounded and released to liquefy the
hillsides; the resulting slurry was sluiced for gold. The landscape produced
by this operation — reddish-ochre spires and ridges of compressed gravel amid
chestnut forests — is one of the most extraordinary industrial landscapes in
Europe. Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1997. Accessible walking
trails through the formation with mirador viewpoints.

## 4. Real Sitio de San Carlos de La Rápita / Sierra de Cartagena-La Unión

The Sierra Minera de Cartagena-La Unión in Murcia produced lead and silver
from Roman times through to 1991, when the last mines closed. Lead-silver-zinc
commodity; closed 1991, Parque Minero de La Unión. The 1998 Aznalcóllar tailings
spill was a separate incident; the Cartagena district has its own major
contamination legacy with heavy metal concentrations in the Mar Menor lagoon
documented since the 1980s.

## 5. Iberian Pyrite Belt

The Iberian Pyrite Belt is a 250-kilometre arc of massive sulphide deposits
stretching from southern Portugal into Huelva and Seville provinces. It
contains some of the largest concentrations of base and precious metals in the
world, worked from prehistoric times through the twentieth century. The belt
includes Río Tinto, Tharsis, La Zarza, Sotiel, and Aznalcóllar. The 1998
Aznalcóllar disaster released tailings from the Boliden Apirsa zinc-copper
mine into the Doñana buffer zone.

## 6. Linares Lead District, Jaén

The Linares-La Carolina district in Jaén province was the world's largest
lead producer in the second half of the nineteenth century, and a significant
employer of British capital and engineers. Lead-silver commodity; principal
production ended 1991. The landscape is studded with ruined engine houses in
a style directly derived from Cornish tin mine architecture — testimony to the
British engineers and miners who worked here from the 1820s. Several headframes
and chimney stacks are protected monuments.

## 7. Cardona Salt Mountain, Catalonia

The Cardona salt diapir is a geological formation of Triassic evaporites that
forms a salt hill above the town of Cardona. Salt commodity; quarried since
prehistoric times, museum operation ongoing. The Cardona Salt Mountain Natural
Park covers the surface exposures, which display spectacular coloured banding
in red, grey, and white. The medieval castle of the Dukes of Cardona, who
controlled the salt revenues, overlooks the site.

## 8. HUNOSA Coal Museums, Asturias

HUNOSA (Hulleras del Norte S.A.) was the state coal company that managed
Asturian coal mines from 1967 to the final closures in the 2010s. Coal
commodity; last collieries closed 2018 under EU state-aid phase-out. Several
HUNOSA-operated museums and heritage sites exist across the Nalón and Caudal
valleys, including the Museo de la Minería y de la Industria (MUMI) at El
Entrego, which has underground tours in original colliery workings and the
finest collection of mining machines in Spain.

## 9. El Carrasco Wolfram Mine, Extremadura

Wolfram (scheelite/wolframite) mines in Extremadura operated under German and
Allied competition during the Second World War, as Spain became a critical
supplier of tungsten to both sides. Tungsten-wolfram commodity; principal
wartime production 1941–1944, mines subsequently worked into the 1980s. Several
mine ruins remain visible in the Sierra de Gata.

## 10. Belmez Coal and Mining Culture, Córdoba

The mining town of Bélmez de la Moraleda in Córdoba province is better known
for the mysterious "faces of Belmez" — images appearing on a house floor —
than for its coal heritage. Coal commodity; Bélmez coalfield operated into the
late twentieth century, now largely closed. The surrounding Peñarroya-Pueblonuevo
area contains the ruins of industrial smelting operations from one of Spain's
major coal and lead-silver districts.

## Planning a Spanish Mining Route

The Río Tinto Parque Minero is reachable from Seville in under two hours and
works well as a half-day excursion. Las Médulas requires a dedicated trip into
the Bierzo from León or Ponferrada; allow a full day including the mirador
walk and the village of Orellán. Almadén sits in central Castilla-La Mancha,
best reached by car from Toledo or Ciudad Real. The HUNOSA museums in Asturias
combine well with the UNESCO pre-Romanesque monuments of Oviedo and the Camino
del Norte. All Spanish mining sites are on the [interactive map](/map).

## The Iberian Pyrite Belt and Rio Tinto as Mars Analogue

The Río Tinto river system has attracted scientific attention far beyond its
mining heritage context. NASA's Astrobiology Institute has studied the river
as an analogue for ancient Mars: the combination of high acidity, high iron
concentrations, and the iron-oxidising bacteria that sustain the system mirrors
conditions that may have existed in Martian aqueous environments more than
3.5 billion years ago. The Tinto Project, operating from the Centro de
Astrobiología (CAB) in Madrid, has drilled into the subsurface of the Iberian
Pyrite Belt to identify microbial communities living in the acidic groundwater
— finding viable micro-organisms at depths of 600 metres. This science sits
directly on top of one of the oldest continuously worked mining landscapes in
the world: the same pyrite that the Phoenicians and Romans extracted for
copper and sulphur is now the substrate for experiments relevant to
extraterrestrial life detection.
