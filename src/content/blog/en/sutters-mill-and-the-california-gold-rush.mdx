On 24 January 1848 James W. Marshall picked a small gold nugget from the tailrace of the sawmill he was building for John Sutter on the American River. The discovery triggered the largest mass migration in 19th-century American history.

## The discovery

Marshall was building a sawmill at Coloma in the Sierra Nevada foothills for John Sutter, owner of Sutter's Fort downstream at Sacramento. The flakes Marshall pulled from the millrace were heavy, malleable and unmistakable.

## Spreading the news

Sutter tried to keep it quiet. By March 1848 word had reached San Francisco; by August it was in the New York Herald; on 5 December President Polk confirmed it in his State of the Union address. The race was on.

## The Forty-Niners

Around 300 000 people arrived in California in 1849 alone. Most came overland by wagon train, by Cape Horn sailing, or via Panama by mule across the isthmus. San Francisco grew from 1000 inhabitants to 25 000 in a single year.

## Mining methods

Initial work was panning and rocker-cradle separation in the streambeds. By 1853 hydraulic mining — high-pressure water blasted at hillsides — caused vast erosion. Hard-rock and dredging operations followed.

## Indigenous and Chinese experience

California's Native Americans were devastated: the Native population fell from perhaps 150 000 to 30 000 between 1846 and 1870. Chinese miners faced violent expulsion and the Foreign Miners' Tax.

## Sutter's collapse

John Sutter lost his land, his cattle and ultimately his sanity to the rush. He died in Washington DC in 1880 still petitioning Congress for compensation.

## Marshall Gold Discovery State Historic Park

The site at Coloma preserves a working replica of the sawmill, Marshall's cabin, the original sawpit and a series of trails. James Marshall is buried on the ridge above.

## Wider gold-rush trails

Highway 49 follows the Mother Lode through Placerville, Sutter Creek, Jackson, Sonora and beyond — a 500-km string of gold-rush towns. Columbia State Historic Park preserves an entire 1850s town.

## Cultural legacy

The Gold Rush gave California statehood (1850), San Francisco its modern identity, and the United States its first major Pacific population centre. The word 'Eureka' became the state motto.
