Mines pose particular challenges for photographers: low light, high contrast, narrow corridors, controlled access. With preparation they reward more than most heritage subjects.

## Equipment

Wide-aperture lens (f/1.8–f/2.8), high-ISO-capable body, no flash where prohibited. Tripods are usually banned in active visitor tours.

## Salt mines

White-balance for warm tungsten lighting; the Chapel of St Kinga (Wieliczka) is one of the most photographed underground spaces in the world.

## Headframes and pithead architecture

Zollverein's Doppelbock and the Quincy Mine hoist are perhaps the most photographed mining structures.

## Open-pit panoramas

Bingham Canyon, Chuquicamata, Hull Rust and Erzberg all reward early-morning low-sun side-lighting from their official viewpoints.

## Ghost towns

Bodie, Calico and Kolmanskop reward dawn and dusk; the cracked-paint and dune-fill aesthetic photographs particularly well in slanting light.

## Tools and machinery

Mine museums often allow close-up tripod work on machinery in the off-hours — ask politely in advance.

## Portraits of guides

Retired miners make remarkable portrait subjects; always ask, and offer to send the photo.

## Industrial-revolution interiors

Beamish, the Black Country Living Museum and the Industriemuseum Henrichshütte all preserve interiors that reward slow exploration.

## Underground rules

Many mines ban any photography in active workings on safety grounds; check in advance.

## Permissions

Always ask staff about commercial use, social-media tagging and sharing.
