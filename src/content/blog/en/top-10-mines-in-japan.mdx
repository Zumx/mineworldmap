Japan's mountainous terrain concealed ore bodies that supported centuries of
feudal and then industrial-era mining. The Meiji-era modernisation from the
1870s used German and British engineers to transform traditional mining into
industrial operations almost overnight. The results produced some of the world's
finest industrial heritage, alongside some of its worst pollution disasters.

## 1. Iwami Ginzan, Shimane Prefecture (UNESCO)

Iwami Ginzan Silver Mine was the largest silver mine in East Asia during the
sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, producing silver that flowed into the
global trade networks of the Spanish and Portuguese empires. Silver commodity;
active 1526–1923, inscribed as UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2007. The site
includes the Ryugenji Mabu shaft (accessible to visitors), the Kumagai family
merchant's house, the refining districts of Yunotsu and Tomogaura, and the
transport routes to the coast. The surrounding landscape of forest, mine shafts,
and merchant town is remarkable for the degree to which it has been preserved.

## 2. Sado Kinzan, Niigata Prefecture

Sado was the most productive gold mine in Japan during the Edo period and the
chief source of gold for the Tokugawa shogunate's currency. Gold commodity;
active 1601–1989, now a museum and UNESCO World Heritage candidate. Two mine
areas are accessible: the Doyu-no-Warito, an enormous V-shaped open cut visible
from a distance, and the Sodayu-ko shaft, where mannequin displays reconstruct
Edo-era working conditions. Sado was inscribed on UNESCO's World Heritage List
in 2024 after a prolonged nomination process complicated by disputes over the
use of Korean forced labour during the Second World War.

## 3. Yubari Coal Mine Museum, Hokkaido

Yubari in central Hokkaido was the centre of Japan's Hokkaido coal industry,
with multiple collieries operating from the Meiji era to 1990. Coal commodity;
last major mine closed 1990. The city declared bankruptcy in 2007 after debts
accumulated partly from failed attempts to build a heritage tourism economy.
The Coal Museum in Yubari includes the only underground coal mine exhibition
in Japan, open from May to October, with an actual descent into former workings.
Yubari's fiscal collapse is studied by local government economists as a case
study in post-industrial transition failure.

## 4. Ashio Copper Mine, Tochigi Prefecture

Ashio was at the centre of Japan's first major industrial pollution disaster.
Copper commodity; operated by Furukawa Zaibatsu from 1877, closed 1973.
From the 1880s onward, sulphur dioxide from the smelters destroyed forests
across the Watarase river basin, and heavy metals contaminated agricultural
land and water. The Ashio Copper Mine Riot of 1907 was the first major
industrial protest in Japan. Today the Ashio Copper Mine Heritage Museum
covers both the technical history and the Tanaka Shozo-led pollution protests
that are considered foundational to Japanese environmental politics.

## 5. Kamioka Mine / Super-Kamiokande, Gifu Prefecture

The Kamioka mine in Gifu Prefecture was a zinc-lead mine operated by Mitsui
Mining & Smelting from 1874 to 2001. Lead-zinc commodity; closed 2001,
converted to physics research. The excavated chambers now house the
Super-Kamiokande neutrino detector — a 50,000-tonne cylindrical tank of
ultrapure water lined with 11,129 photomultiplier tubes, 1,000 metres underground.
It detected neutrinos from Supernova 1987A and contributed to the Nobel
Prize-winning discovery of neutrino oscillation. The adjacent XMASS dark matter
detector is also housed in former mine workings.

## 6. Innai Silver Mine, Akita Prefecture

Innai (Niida) silver mine in Akita was discovered in 1606 and operated through
the Edo and Meiji periods. Silver commodity; closed 1954. The site is preserved
as the Innai Mine History Museum with accessible underground shafts. Akita
Prefecture was historically one of Japan's most important mining prefectures,
with gold, silver, and copper deposits distributed across its mountains.

## 7. Hosokura Lead-Zinc Mine, Miyagi Prefecture

Hosokura was one of Japan's principal lead-zinc producers, operated by
Mitsubishi Mining from 1874 to 1987. Lead-zinc commodity; permanently closed.
The Hosokura Mine History Museum preserves surface infrastructure and documents
the history of the Sanriku coastal mining region.

## 8. Akabira Coal Mining Museum, Hokkaido

Akabira, in the Sorachi coalfield, preserves the Bibai Mine headframe and
associated surface infrastructure, now housing a museum covering the Sorachi
coalfield's history. Coal commodity; principal operations closed by 1972. The
Sorachi coalfield, which once produced a third of Japan's coal, is one of
Hokkaido's most complete coal heritage landscapes.

## 9. Hanaoka Mine, Akita Prefecture

Hanaoka is the site of the Hanaoka Incident of 1945, in which several hundred
Chinese forced labourers were killed following a desperate uprising against
their conditions. Copper-zinc commodity; operated by Fujita Mining, closed 1994.
The massacre and its aftermath — the bodies were concealed, Japanese executives
were prosecuted in 1948 and then pardoned — was the subject of prolonged legal
proceedings by survivors and their descendants into the 1990s. The site has a
memorial; the heritage context is inseparable from this history.

## 10. Osarizawa Copper Mine, Akita Prefecture

Osarizawa has been mined for copper since the early seventeenth century, with
significant Meiji-era development. Copper commodity; closed 1978, now a show
mine with underground tours on an electric mine train through 1.5 kilometres
of galleries. The museum displays both Edo-period hand tools and Meiji
industrial machinery. The site is one of the best-preserved examples of the
transitional mining era in Japan.

## Meiji Modernisation and Industrial Heritage

The Meiji Restoration of 1868 opened Japan to foreign technical expertise, and
mining was among the first industries modernised. The government hired German
mine engineers from Freiberg (Bergakademie Freiberg) to transform silver, copper,
and coal operations. The Heritage of Industrial Revolution in the Meiji Era, a
group of twenty-three sites inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2015,
includes the Miike Coal Mines in Fukuoka and the Hashima Island (Battleship
Island) undersea coal mine — both significant for the use of forced Korean
labour during the Second World War, a complication that shaped the inscription
process.

## The Ashio Pollution Legacy and Environmental History

Japan's first modern environmental protest centred on the Ashio copper mine and
its impact on the Watarase River basin from the 1880s onward. Tanaka Shozo, a
member of the National Diet, resigned his seat in 1901 to lead the affected
farming communities directly, and petitioned the Meiji Emperor in person in
a dramatic act of protest. The Yanaka village, in the floodplain of the
contaminated river, was compulsorily purchased and inundated in 1907, and its
former residents became Japan's first environmental refugees. The Yanaka ruins —
flooded, then re-exposed during droughts — are now a scheduled heritage site.
The Ashio-Tanaka Shozo Museum covers this history in the town of Tsuruda.

## Planning a Japanese Mining Route

Iwami Ginzan in western Shimane Prefecture is the most rewarding single
destination for an international mining visitor: UNESCO status, multiple
accessible shafts and merchant districts, and a preserved rural landscape.
It combines well with the Izumo Taisha and the Adachi Museum of Art on the San'in
coast. Sado requires a ferry from Niigata — allow two days minimum for the
Doyu-no-Warito, the Sodayu-ko underground tour, and the island's Noh cultural
heritage. The Kamioka-Super Kamiokande site requires advance institutional
permission to visit the underground detector, but the mine museum above ground
is accessible. All Japanese sites are on the [interactive map](/map), where
you can filter by commodity and UNESCO status.
